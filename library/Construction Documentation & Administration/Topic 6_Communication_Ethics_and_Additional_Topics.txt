TOPIC: PROFESSIONAL COMMUNICATION

Active listening:
- Focusing on what is being said AND how it is being said
- Asking follow-up questions to fully understand the message BEFORE formulating a response
- Withholding judgment until the speaker has finished
- Distinguished from passive hearing by full engagement with the speaker
- Particularly important in client meetings where understanding true goals and concerns is essential

Communication methods used on construction projects to share knowledge and evaluate outcomes:
- Pre-construction meetings where the project is explained
- Paper trail diary
- Proposal or contract
- Material specifications
- Schedule (project management report)
- Financial information and vendor contracts
- Project status reports
- Signoff forms
- Letters, memos, and meeting agendas
- Conversations
- Interviews, faxes, and photos
- Computer software programs
- SMEE-mail archives
- Checklist and punch list
- Legal documents such as deeds, permits, and covenants
- Telephone hotline available at all hours of the day and night
- Key people such as project team, client representatives, and outside organizations
- Soils tests

Best practices for professional meetings:
- Every meeting should have a clearly defined purpose and desired outcome established before it begins
- An agenda should be distributed to all participants prior to the meeting and followed during it
- Meetings should start and end on time
- Meetings should not exceed one hour unless absolutely necessary
- Someone should be designated to take notes and distribute minutes after the meeting
- Each item on the agenda should have a time limit
- Set ground rules at the beginning
- Include only individuals who can contribute to the discussions and decisions
- Make the meeting formal
- Move quickly through topics
- Schedule breaks as needed
- Lead the meeting effectively

Techniques for facilitating meetings include:
- Start on time and finish on time — each item on the agenda should have a time limit
- Set ground rules
- Include the individuals who can contribute to the meetings discussions and decisions
- Make sure the meeting is formal
- Move quickly through topics
- Schedule breaks
- Lead the meeting

Techniques for group discussion include:
- Discursive approach: discussion is allowed to flow and individuals decide whether they want to participate
- Round robin approach: each member of the group is given the opportunity to speak in turn
- Small group approach: the group breaks into smaller groups focusing on one agenda item

The two most commonly used approaches to reach a group decision:
- Consensus approach
- Majority vote approach

Meeting minutes:
- The official record of what discussions occurred at the meeting and what decisions were made
- Record the action plan — tasks assigned, timelines for completion, and the person responsible for each task
- Minutes serve as the project's memory for decisions made during meetings

Best practices for email communication:
- Use email to confirm discussions or decisions that have already taken place — creates a written record of verbal agreements
- Use email to transmit lengthy documents as attachments
- Do NOT use email for sensitive conversations, complex negotiations, or delivering bad news — those require face-to-face or phone communication
- Email threads should not substitute for meetings when a decision requires back-and-forth discussion

Synergy (described by Mary Follett in 1918):
- The process by which the effect of a group is more than the sum of its parts
- A well-functioning collaborative team produces better work than the same individuals working independently
- Achieved through effective communication, mutual respect, and shared commitment to project goals

The charrette process:
- An intensive collaborative design process conducted on site over multiple days with a multidisciplinary team
- Word origin: French word for cart — historically referring to the cart that carried student work to the Ecole des Beaux-Arts for review
- Three defining characteristics:
  1. Conducted ON SITE and lasts MULTIPLE DAYS
  2. Teams are MULTIDISCIPLINARY so that integrated solutions addressing multiple design aspects simultaneously are produced
  3. Each work session is followed by a FEEDBACK DISCUSSION with stakeholders — the design evolves iteratively based on community or client input

Offshoring:
- The practice of using service providers located OUTSIDE of North America to perform production work
- Typically drafting, CAD, or BIM work
- Performed at a lower cost or faster turnaround
- Distinct from domestic outsourcing
- Requires careful quality control and coordination
- Both cost and time zone advantages are factors

TOPIC: PROFESSIONAL ETHICS

The landscape architect's code of ethics addresses these areas to avoid ethical dilemmas:

Responsibility to the public:
- Make every reasonable effort to protect the safety, health, property, and welfare of the public

Public statements, reports, and testimony:
- Express opinion based on knowledge of the material facts
- Background of technical competence in the subject matter is required

Competency for assignments undertaken or approved:
- Undertake assignments ONLY when qualified to do so by education or experience, or both

Conflicts of interest:
- Avoid conflicts of interest with the employer or client

Improper solicitation of professional employment:
- Do not compensate, give anything of value, or offer to compensate or give anything of value to secure employment

Knowledge of improper conduct by others:
- Do not conceal, refuse to divulge, or make false or misleading statements about the matter

Seals and rubber stamps:
- Affixed name or seal ONLY to any drawing, specification, or other document prepared by licensees or under personal supervision

Standards of Practice:
- The guiding principles by which licensees conduct their day-to-day responsibilities within their scope of practice

Reasonable care and skill:
- Any work done must be at least as good as the work of a competent person with average skills and experience in the type of work required

According to Manufacturers Specifications:
- The written installation and/or maintenance instructions developed by the manufacturer of a product
- Which may have to be followed in order to maintain the product's warranty

Written warranty — two general types in construction:
- Manufacturer warranty of a product (example: Patio furniture warranty)
- Labor warranty (example: roofing contract may include a 30-year material warranty AND a 5-year labor warranty)

TOPIC: PROFESSIONAL LICENSING

State licensing laws pertaining to landscape architecture:

Title Act:
- Allows only qualified individuals to call themselves a Landscape Architect
- An individual may still PERFORM the work of a landscape architect but not identify themselves as a landscape architect
- Less restrictive form of regulation

Practice Act:
- Prohibits an unqualified individual from BOTH calling themselves a Landscape Architect AND from practicing the profession
- It is the STRONGEST form of regulation to ensure the protection of the public health, safety, and welfare

Sunset law reviews:
- The periodic review of state agencies that exercise the state's regulatory authority over occupations
- Agencies are terminated by specified dates unless their life is extended by legislative action

Reciprocal licensure:
- A policy which acknowledges and accepts credentials awarded by another authority or jurisdiction
- Allows a landscape architect to serve a client in another state
- Also called reciprocity

The practice of landscape architecture is licensed in:
- 47 U.S. states
- One U.S. territory
- Two Canadian provinces

CLARB (Council of Landscape Architectural Registration Boards):
- The body that administers the LARE examination
- Once licensed in any state, a landscape architect can establish a record with CLARB
- This can expedite the reciprocity process when applying for licensure in other states

Professional liability — courts apply the term design professional primarily to:
- Architects
- Engineers
- Landscape architects
- Surveyors

The standard courts use to evaluate professional performance:
- The law imposes upon persons performing architectural, engineering, and other professional and skilled services the obligation to exercise a reasonable degree of care, skill, and ability
- This standard means the work must be at least as good as the work of a competent person with average skills and experience in similar circumstances and like surrounding circumstances
- Design professionals are held to a standard of care consistent with the minimum standard of care in their profession
- Expert testimony must usually be used to establish this standard of care

Design professional liability can be limited:
- A design professional can limit liability to a client largely through the contract between the design professional and the client
- The contract typically sets forth the design professional's duties and, to a lesser extent, how they are to be carried out

TOPIC: TEAM BUILDING AND LEADERSHIP

Techniques for building a strong team include:
- Establishing objectives together: define performance objectives with the team and make sure all team members understand the objectives and what actions will need to be taken to achieve them
- Developing a participatory style: encourage staff to suggest ways to improve services, listen to their ideas, acknowledge their points of view, and encourage team members to discuss issues and find solutions together
- Focusing on contributions: have all team members actively contribute to the meeting
- Organizing meetings with the whole team to encourage them to discuss their concerns
- Organizing the team by defining roles and responsibilities together
- Explaining the rules that have been established for the project
- Promoting team responsibility: encouraging members to take responsibility for completing specific tasks and solving problems as a team
- Establishing time commitments: scheduling when and how each team member will devote time to team work

Motivation to Manage (MTM) factor includes:
- Favorable attitude toward authority
- Desire to compete
- Desire to exercise power
- Desire for a distinctive position
- A sense of responsibility
NOTE: All five of these elements are part of the MTM factor — exam questions may ask which are included.

TOPIC: BUSINESS PRACTICE AND MARKETING

Grant proposals:
- The word "grant" refers to a sum of money given to support the work of an agency, organization, or individual
- Usually as a result of a formal decision-making process involving a written or oral presentation and review
- A Facade and Landscapes Rehabilitation Grant awards monies to rehabilitate facades and landscapes

Promotional materials:
- Printed materials, brochures, portfolios, and other printed pieces to describe services
- Advertising, paid marketing via newspapers, periodicals, the phone book, on television or radio, or your own web site
- Often the most costly form of marketing

Referrals:
- An effective and inexpensive way to increase business and build credibility
- Ask clients or suppliers for contacts of other people who might like to find out more about your business

Daily construction report:
- A written record of the day's activities
- Weather conditions
- Work accomplished
- Number of personnel for each trade involved
- Equipment involved
- Materials received
- Visitors or inspectors present
- Problems encountered

TOPIC: DISPUTE RESOLUTION

Methods of dispute resolution in construction contracts:

Arbitration:
- A formal process in which parties agree to give up their right to go to court
- Disputes are resolved by an arbitrator or arbitration panel
- Binding arbitration: parties agree to accept the arbitrator's decision as final; the arbitrator's decision cannot be appealed
- Nonbinding arbitration: parties are free not to accept the arbitrator's decision and may seek satisfaction through other means including a lawsuit
- Typically faster and less expensive than litigation
- An arbitration clause in a contract is a contractual provision whereby those bound to it give up their right to go to court

Mediation:
- Parties agree to enter into good faith negotiations
- Through a neutral mediator
- In order to attempt to resolve their differences
- Voluntary and non-binding — does NOT produce a binding decision
- Distinguished from arbitration: mediation results in a negotiated agreement if successful, not a binding ruling

Litigation:
- Going to court
- Most expensive and time-consuming dispute resolution method
- Used when other methods have failed or are not agreed to by both parties

A contract clause outlines how any disputes will be resolved and most include:
- An arbitration agreement
- A mediation provision for good faith negotiations through a neutral mediator

TOPIC: LEGAL CONCEPTS IN CONSTRUCTION CONTRACTS

Latin legal terms tested on the LARE:

Bona fide:
- Meaning: in good faith
- All parties to a construction contract are expected to perform their obligations in good faith

Res ipsa loquitur:
- Meaning: the thing speaks for itself
- Used in negligence cases where circumstances so obviously indicate negligence that detailed proof is unnecessary

Stare decisis:
- Meaning: to stand by past decisions
- The legal doctrine of following precedent — courts refer to prior rulings when deciding similar cases

Condemnation:
- The legal term for the exercise of eminent domain
- The government's power to seize private property for public use
- Just compensation must be paid to the owner

Estoppel:
- A legal doctrine that prevents a person from denying or asserting a fact due to a previous act or statement
- In construction contracts: arises when a party has acted in a way another party has relied upon, preventing the first party from later taking a contradictory position

Privity of contract:
- The direct legal relationship between parties to a contract
- The owner has privity with the general contractor
- The general contractor has privity with subcontractors
- The owner typically does NOT have privity with subcontractors
- This affects who can sue whom for breach of contract

Indemnification clauses:
- One-way indemnity clause: requires one party (typically the designer) to indemnify and defend the owner against ALL claims — not just those caused by the designer's negligence; extremely broad, shifts significant risk onto the designer; landscape architects should be wary of these
- Mutual or shared indemnification clause: both parties agree to hold each other harmless for their respective negligence; more equitable and the preferred arrangement
- Hold harmless agreement: similar to mutual indemnification — both parties agree to hold each other harmless in the event of a problem; can be combined with an arbitration clause

Elements required for a valid contract — all four must be present:
1. An offer — one party proposes terms
2. Acceptance — the other party agrees to those terms without modification
3. Consideration — something of value exchanged by both parties (typically money for services or construction)
4. Legal capacity — all parties must be of legal age and sound mind

TOPIC: CONSTRUCTION DOCUMENTS — ADDITIONAL LEGAL CONTEXT

A legal set of contract documents comprises:
- Working drawings
- Specifications
- General conditions
- Form of agreement
NOTE: All four of these together make a legal set — not just drawings, not just specifications.

The cover sheet of a set of construction plans normally includes:
- Client names
- Consultant names
- Property legal description
- Vicinity map
- Project name

When writing a legal contract with an owner for construction phase services, terms to avoid in reference to the landscape architect's role:
- Supervision (implies control over contractor's work)
- Inspection (implies control and responsibility for correctness)
NOTE: These terms can create liability. The correct terms are observation and administration.

The primary role of regulatory agencies during the construction phase:
- Verify compliance with codes, ordinances, and regulations
- NOT to provide oversight on behalf of the landscape architect
- NOT to collect permit fees (that happens during permitting, not construction)

The primary job of the landscape architect during the construction observation phase:
- Ensure compliance with construction documents
NOTE: NOT to review contract labor laws, NOT to instruct on methods of construction, NOT to address change orders exclusively

TOPIC: ADDITIONAL CONSTRUCTION ADMINISTRATION CONCEPTS

Substantial completion:
- The status when the project is sufficiently finished to be occupied by the owner
- Against which the project duration is measured
- The owner can begin using the facility for its intended purpose

Final completion:
- When all work is done in full conformance with the contract documents
- Triggers release of all remaining retainage
- Triggers final payment

Punch list:
- Developed at the time of substantial completion
- Itemizes all remaining work tasks that must be performed before final completion

Retainage:
- A stated percentage of the progress payment request withheld by the owner as incentive for the contractor to complete the project
- Generally returned after final completion

Notice to Proceed:
- The date on which the owner gives written authorization instructing a design firm or contractor to begin work
- Can happen before the contract is finalized

Redlines:
- Drawings that have been reviewed by senior staff and marked up with changes and revisions
- The term comes from the red pen traditionally used for review markups
- An internal quality control tool

Shop drawings vs. submittals:
- Shop drawings: information concerning products to be incorporated in a construction project prepared by the contractor or fabricator showing how items will be manufactured and installed
- Submittals: the broader category that includes shop drawings, product data, samples, certifications, and other contractor-provided documents for review

Differing site conditions:
- Physical conditions on a site that differ from what was shown in the bidding documents
- OR conditions that differ from what would reasonably be expected at the site
- Do NOT always result in termination of the contract — that is a false statement; they are addressed through the change order process

Types of construction delays and consequences:
- Owner-caused delays: generally result in BOTH contract duration extensions AND possible contract amount adjustments
- Excusable delays (acts of God): typically result in TIME EXTENSIONS ONLY
- Contractor-caused delays: result in neither a time extension nor additional compensation; owner may assess liquidated damages

Actual acceleration vs. constructive acceleration:
- Actual acceleration: an increase in construction pace resulting from a SPECIFIC DIRECTIVE from the owner
- Constructive acceleration: an increase in pace NOT from a directive but by inference — such as when a legitimate request for a time extension is denied; this is done without a direct order

Fast-tracking: a delivery method where design and construction phases OVERLAP to compress the schedule — NOT the same as acceleration of construction pace

Addenda:
- Formal changes or clarifications issued to ALL identified bidders by the owner during the bidding period
- Plural of addendum
- Become part of the contract documents

Change order:
- A directive usually authorized in writing by the owner to alter or modify some aspect of a project
- Generally accompanied by an adjustment to the contract amount and/or duration

Field change (field order):
- An authorized directive by the owner's representative to alter or modify some aspect of a project
- Normally NOT accompanied by any change in contract amount or duration
- For minor conditions that can be resolved without financial or schedule impact

Commissioning:
- The process of testing and checking all equipment and systems within a facility at the end of a project
- To assure proper functioning and operation

Value Engineering:
- A review and analysis of a project by a third-party team of experts
- Mission: improve performance, reliability, quality, safety, and life-cycle costs

Program Validation:
- The design team performing an independent check of the client's stated program and budget before beginning work
- Identifying any cost increases or unanticipated required tasks

Design to Cost:
- A method of design where the initial task sets an appropriate cost target
- All subsequent planning and design activities are monitored for conformance with that target

Shell package:
- Designed space delivered in a basic unfinished condition
- Landlord provides the building structure and core systems but NOT the interior build-out

Tenant improvement package:
- Design and construction work paid for by tenants who wish to design their own interiors
- Owner may provide a site with a complete exterior and landscape built under a separate contract

Total Quality Management (TQM) — three components:
- Managing the quality of the design AND the design process
- Upper management leading by example
- Assuming employees will naturally do a good job if properly trained and given correct tools
NOTE: TQM does NOT focus quality efforts exclusively on final deliverables — it applies throughout the entire process.

TOPIC: SPECIFICATIONS AND STANDARDS ORGANIZATIONS

Organizations and their functions — know these distinctions:

CSI (Construction Specifications Institute):
- Publishes specification standards and the MasterFormat
- Does NOT publish cost estimating data

ASTM (American Society for Testing and Materials):
- Publishes material testing standards
- Does NOT publish cost estimating data

ANSI (American National Standards Institute):
- Covers general safety and performance standards

ANLA (American Nursery and Landscape Association):
- Sets the standards for health, size, shape, and vigor of plant materials
- The governing body recognized for setting standards for plant materials, earthwork, compaction, and specification writing

RS Means:
- Publishes cost estimating reference manuals for the United States

Saylor:
- Publishes cost estimating reference manuals for the United States

AIA (American Institute of Architects):
- Produces standard contract forms widely used in the industry
- AIA standard forms have been refined over decades and address most common risk scenarios

CLARB (Council of Landscape Architectural Registration Boards):
- Administers the LARE examination
- Maintains records for reciprocal licensure

OSHA (Occupational Safety and Health Administration):
- Sets workplace safety standards
- OSHA minimum railing height for decks and platforms: 42 inches
- Maximum spacing between railing members: 4 inches (based on size of a baby's head)

CPSC (Consumer Product Safety Commission):
- Sets guidelines for pool barriers and playground equipment safety

USGBC (US Green Building Council):
- Developed the LEED rating system (Leadership in Energy and Environmental Design)

ADA (Americans with Disabilities Act):
- Governs accessibility requirements for landscape architecture
- Applies to playground accessibility, wayfinding, and public spaces

AASHTO (American Association of State Highway and Transportation Officials):
- Deals with road and highway construction standards
- The AASHTO Green Book is the primary highway design reference
- AASHTO requires cross-slope of roads to be at least 1.5 percent to permit adequate drainage

USDA (United States Department of Agriculture):
- USDA plant hardiness zone map gives a general indication of which plant species will survive in a given climate zone
- USDA soil classification system is the standard for evaluating soils for planting conditions in the United States

TOPIC: LANDSCAPE MAINTENANCE AND POST-CONSTRUCTION

Landscape maintenance materials include:
- Edging: materials used to separate landscape areas from other areas; forms include treated wood, stone, concrete, or plastic
- Weed barrier: woven fabric that inhibits growth of weeds in landscape areas
- Mulch materials: act to condition the soil and inhibit the growth of weeds in landscape areas
- Ground cover materials: a variety of materials used for covering fabric weed barrier; also a variety of mulch materials and rock materials
- Root guard protection materials
- Stepping stones and pavers

Types of landscape equipment used for maintenance:
- Leaf blowers for blowing leaves and vacuuming leaves
- Chain saws for trimming
- Hedge trimmers
- Lawn mowers — manual, hand pushed, and ride-on/catchers
- Mulching lawn mowers, tractors, and tractor-mounted equipment
- String trimmers to trim areas the mower cannot reach
- Pruning shears for cutting and trimming branches
- Snow blowers, aerators, and sprayers
- Lawn edgers and rollers
- Power rakes and thatchers
- Tillers and weed trimmers
- Top dressers
- Compact equipment: skid steer loaders, mini excavators, compact tractor loader backhoes, small wheeled loaders, and telescopic rough-terrain forklifts
- Hand-held tools: shovels, sweepers, rakes, clippers, cutters, and wheelbarrows

Basic landscape maintenance practices:
- Aeration: improving the gas and air exchange capabilities of soil
- Fertilization: supplemental application of nutrients required in the soil for healthy and sustained plant growth
- Mowing operations
- Mulching: placement of organic material over a plant's root zone
- Pest and disease control
- Pruning: selective removal of foliage or branches
- Soil amendments: any materials added to the soil to improve or maintain its texture, pH, or friability
- Trimming: removal of excess or unwanted turfgrass or similar plant material
- Weed control

Landscape maintenance techniques that should follow appropriate recommendations:
- Managing irrigation and runoff
- Watering according to plant needs
- Fertilizing according to a soil analysis
- Pruning to shape a plant, allow air circulation, or remove dead, diseased, or damaged tissue
- Aeration of mulched beds and turf areas to ensure roots are healthy and anaerobic areas do not develop in mulched beds
- Mowing of lawn areas
- Storing and disposing of gardening wastes properly
- Mulching or other erosion control measures on exposed soils
- Managing pesticide and herbicide use properly
- Incorporating pest management techniques where appropriate

TOPIC: FEDERAL AND REGULATORY CONTEXT

Federal acts relevant to landscape architecture practice:
- Federal Water Pollution Control Act of 1972 (FWPCA / Clean Water Act): governs water pollution
- Clean Water Act: allows states to establish clean water programs
- National Pollutant Discharge Elimination System (NPDES): controls water pollution by regulating point and nonpoint sources that discharge pollutants into waters of the United States
- Endangered Species Act: governs how animal and plant species decline or close to extinction will be protected and recovered
- National Environmental Policy Act (NEPA): ensures the integration of natural and social sciences and environmental design in planning and decision-making for federal projects or projects on federal lands
- Safe Drinking Water Act (SDWA): regulates the treatment of water for human consumption
- Watershed Protection and Flood Prevention Act (PL 83-566): authorizes states and local agencies to regulate soil conservation, flood prevention, conservation, development, utilization, and disposal of water
- Wellhead Protection Program: amendment to the Federal Safe Drinking Water Act in 1986

The Environmental Protection Agency (EPA):
- Established in 1970 to oversee various federal environmental laws that had been enacted
- One major function: review the environmental impacts of highway projects, large-scale commercial and residential construction, power plants, and other large undertakings involving the federal government
- A major tool: the power to issue an environmental impact statement that evaluates a proposed project before it is undertaken

TOPIC: POOL AND BARRIER DESIGN STANDARDS

US Consumer Product Safety Commission pool barrier guidelines:
- Pool top barrier must be at least 48 inches above grade, measured on the side of the barrier which faces away from the swimming pool
- Eliminate handholds and footholds
- Minimize the size of openings in a barrier's construction — all openings should be small enough that a 4-inch diameter sphere cannot pass through (based on head breadth and chest depth of a young child)
- Barrier bottom should not exceed 4-inch maximum clearance above grade, when the measurement is done on the side of the barrier facing away from the pool
- Barriers should be equipped with a gate or gates which restrict access to the pool
- A locking device should be included in the gate design
- Gates should open OUT from the pool
- Gates should be self-closing and self-latching

TOPIC: QUICK REFERENCE — NUMBERS AND FACTS TO MEMORIZE

Code dimensions and thresholds:
- OSHA minimum railing height for decks and platforms: 42 inches above adjacent deck surface
- Maximum spacing between railing members: 4 inches (based on size of a baby's head)
- Maximum planting height in site triangle at 35 mph road: 30 inches
- Maximum height a landscape architect can design a retaining wall without engineer: 3 feet
- DBH measurement height: 4.5 feet above grade
- Concrete reaches full design strength: 28 days
- Minimum depth of contraction joint in concrete slab: 1/6 the depth of the slab
- Best planting season: Fall
- Stakes and guy wires: loosen after 1st growing season, remove after 2nd growing season
- Maximum topsoil stockpile depth: 4 feet
- Neutral soil pH range: 6.6 to 7.3
- Retainage percentage: typically 5 to 10 percent
- Guarantee period: typically 1 year from substantial completion
- Design contingency: typically 5 to 20 percent of total budget
- Pool barrier height minimum: 48 inches above grade
- Pool barrier maximum gap size: 4 inches diameter sphere
- Addenda minimum notice period before bid day: typically 3 to 7 days
- Irrigation sleeve installation: after mass earthwork, before any permanent hardscape
- Number 4 rebar diameter: 1/2 inch (or 1.25 cm)

Organization functions for the exam:
- CSI: specification standards and MasterFormat — NOT cost estimating
- ASTM: material testing standards — NOT cost estimating
- RS Means and Saylor: cost estimating reference manuals
- ANLA: plant material health, size, shape, and vigor standards
- CLARB: administers the LARE exam
- OSHA: workplace safety standards including railing heights
- CPSC: pool barrier and playground equipment safety guidelines
- USGBC: LEED rating system
- USDA: plant hardiness zone map and soil classification system for agricultural and planting conditions
- ADA: accessibility requirements

MasterFormat key divisions (2004 edition):

- Division 31: Earthwork (topsoil stripping and stockpiling)

- Division 33: Utilities

Contract types summary:
- Lump Sum (Stipulated Sum): fixed price regardless of actual costs — risk on contractor
- Time and Materials (T&M): paid for actual costs — no cap — risk on owner
- T&M with Cap (Not-to-Exceed): T&M with a maximum — protects owner
- Guaranteed Maximum Price (GMP): cost-basis billing with a maximum — contractor absorbs overruns
- Cost Plus Fixed Fee: actual costs reimbursed plus fixed profit that does not change

Delivery methods summary:
- General Contract (design-bid-build): designer and contractor have separate contracts with owner
- Design-Build: one entity contracts for both design and construction
- Professional Construction Management: CM firm represents owner throughout design and construction
- Multiple Prime Contracts: owner holds separate contracts with multiple prime contractors

Delay consequences summary:
- Owner-caused: contractor gets TIME EXTENSION + ADDITIONAL COMPENSATION (time and money)
- Excusable (force majeure): contractor gets TIME EXTENSION ONLY (time, no money)
- Contractor-caused: contractor gets NOTHING; owner may assess liquidated damages

Bond types summary:
- Bid bond: submitted with bid; guarantees bidder will execute contract if awarded
- Performance bond: guarantees contractor will complete the work
- Payment bond: guarantees contractor will pay all subs, suppliers, and laborers

Submittal response designations:
- Approved: proceed as shown
- Approved as Noted: proceed with noted modifications incorporated
- Revise and Resubmit: substantially revise and resubmit for another review
- Rejected: fundamentally unacceptable
