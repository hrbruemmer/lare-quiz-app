TOPIC: REQUIRED PLAN SHEETS

A complete landscape architecture construction document set includes these standard sheet types:
- Cover sheet
- Existing conditions plan (also called base plan or survey plan)
- Demolition plan
- Layout plan (also called layout and staking plan)
- Materials plan (also called paving plan or hardscape plan)
- Grading and drainage plan
- Planting plan
- Irrigation plan
- Construction details
- Supplemental plans as needed (lighting, playground, wayfinding, site furnishings)

Cover sheet must include:
- Project name and address
- Client name and contact information
- Names and contact information of all consultants
- Property legal description
- Vicinity map showing project location in broader context
- Complete list of all drawings in the set organized by sheet number and title
- Permit information
- Applicable code references
- Project north arrow and graphic scale

Why the legal description on the cover sheet matters:
- Permitting agencies use the legal description to locate the property in their records
- A missing or incorrect legal description can delay or block the permit process
- It is critical for the contractor and for any future work on the site

Existing conditions plan documents:
- Everything that currently exists on the site
- Buildings and structures
- Trees with species and size noted
- Utilities both above-ground and below-ground
- Existing grades and topographic contours
- Pavement and hardscape
- Property lines and easements
- Drainage features such as swales and inlets
- Environmentally sensitive areas such as wetlands or floodplains
NOTE: Prepared from a topographic survey by a licensed surveyor.

Demolition plan elements:
- Items to be removed — shown with dashed lines or an X through them
- Items to remain — shown at a lighter line weight
- Items to be protected — called out with protective symbols and specific notes
- Common elements include: existing trees to be removed, pavement to be removed or sawcut, structures to be demolished, utilities to be capped or abandoned, fences and walls to be removed

Sawcut definition and purpose:
- A clean straight cut made in existing pavement at the limit of demolition
- Maintains the structural integrity of the remaining pavement
- Creates a neat edge for new construction to meet
- Prevents ragged edges that can crack and deteriorate

Critical coordination requirement:
- The demolition plan must coordinate precisely with the protection and mitigation plan
- Any discrepancy between what the demolition plan says gets removed and what the protection plan says gets saved creates field confusion
- Discrepancies can generate contractor claims for additional time or cost

TOPIC: PROTECTION AND MITIGATION PLANS

The protection and mitigation plan identifies all existing features that must survive the construction process and specifies protective measures required for each.

Tree protection measures — in order of importance:
- Install construction barrier fencing at the dripline of the tree (MOST IMPORTANT)
- Apply wood chip mulch within the protection zone
- Install protective signage
- Install temporary irrigation during construction if needed
- Include monetary damage provisions in contract documents

The dripline:
- The outer edge of the tree's canopy
- Corresponds roughly to the outer extent of the root system below ground
- The correct location for protective fencing

Construction barrier fencing requirements:
- Must be installed BEFORE any site work begins — before clearing, demolition, or grading
- Inside the fenced zone: no equipment may operate
- Inside the fenced zone: no materials may be stored
- Inside the fenced zone: no soil may be compacted
- Inside the fenced zone: no grade changes may occur

DBH — Diameter Breast Height:
- Measured at 4.5 feet above grade
- Standard measurement for specifying tree size in construction documents
- Trees failing to meet specified DBH should be rejected from the project

Why grade changes near trees are dangerous:
- Raising the grade over roots cuts off oxygen to the root system
- Lowering the grade cuts roots directly
- Both can kill an otherwise healthy tree
- Even minor changes within the dripline can cause fatal damage

A proper tree protection note specifies:
- The species
- The DBH
- The protection radius
- The required fencing type
- The financial consequences if the tree is damaged by the contractor
NOTE: A vague note such as "protect existing tree" is insufficient.

Beyond trees, the protection and mitigation plan addresses:
- Adjacent buildings and structures on neighboring properties
- Active utilities that remain in service
- Existing hardscape to remain in place
- Environmentally sensitive areas
- Stormwater features that must remain functional during construction

TOPIC: LAYOUT AND MATERIALS PLANS

Layout plan content:
- Precise horizontal location of all new site elements
- Paths, plazas, walls, steps, ramps, structures, planters, site furnishings
- Every element dimensioned from known reference points
- Key elevations including finished floor elevations and spot elevations at grade transitions
- Top and bottom of wall elevations
- Must coordinate with the grading and drainage plan

Two primary methods for locating elements on a layout plan:

Dimensioning method:
- Measures distances from fixed reference points such as property lines or existing buildings
- Works well for rectilinear designs
- Simpler to produce and read

Coordinate method (also called staking method):
- Locates elements using a northings and eastings coordinate system
- Contractor uses surveying equipment or GPS
- Preferred for large or complex projects
- Preferred for curved geometry that is difficult to dimension conventionally
- Creates a precise, unambiguous location for every element

Materials plan content:
- Designates the surface material for every area of the site
- Uses hatching, keynotes, and a legend
- Shows what material goes where in plan view
- Includes a legend listing every material with its hatch pattern
- Keynotes reference each material
- References to detail sheets showing each material assembly in cross-section
- Transition notes at material edges showing how one material meets another
- Notes about special conditions such as scored patterns or inlaid materials

Critical relationship between materials plan and construction details:
- The materials plan shows WHAT material goes WHERE in plan view
- The construction details show HOW each material assembly is BUILT in cross-section
- Both must be perfectly coordinated — conflicts are expensive to resolve in the field

TOPIC: CONSTRUCTION DETAILS — PURPOSE AND DRAWING METHODS

Construction details show how individual elements are built in cross-section, elevation, or three dimensions.

The primary drawing method used in construction details:
- Orthographic drawing
- Presents a design from multiple two-dimensional perspectives
- Includes plan view, elevation view, and section view
- Allows a three-dimensional assembly to be fully communicated through flat drawings
- Construction detail sheets communicate through dimensions and orthographic drawings
- NOT through isometric or perspective views

Types of construction details:
- Wall details — cross-section from footing to top
- Paving details — cross-section from subgrade to surface
- Drainage details — how water is captured and conveyed
- Planting details — how plants are installed
- Structure details — how pergolas, arbors, site furnishings are built and connected
- Green roof details — the layered assembly of a vegetated roof system

Wall detail content:
- Footing dimensions, depth, material, and reinforcement
- Wall material and thickness
- Any reinforcement within the wall body
- Drainage features such as weep holes or perforated pipe
- Backfill material specification
- Waterproofing or dampproofing on back face
- Cap or coping detail at the top

Paving detail content:
- Subgrade — native soil with compaction requirement
- Base course — typically crushed aggregate with depth and compaction requirement
- Any intermediate layers such as sand setting bed for pavers
- Wearing surface — finished material with thickness and finish specified
- Edge conditions — curbs, edging, or transition details
- Joint spacing and treatment

Drainage detail content:
- Dimensions and material of drainage structures such as catch basins and trench drains
- Pipe material, size, and slope
- How drainage connects to the overall stormwater system
- Filter fabric and aggregate layers in subsurface systems

TOPIC: SOILS

Three soil property categories always considered when planning and designing a site:
- Physical properties
- Site engineering properties
- Horticultural properties

The three main components that make up soil:
- Silt
- Sand
- Clay
NOTE: Bark, mulch, and other organic materials are additives, not soil components.

Neutral soil pH range: 6.6 to 7.3

Soil pH affects:
- Plant survival
- The color of some plants
- The durability of landscape construction materials such as rock and plastics

Urban soil failure causes and consequences:
- Caused by overcompaction and poor maintenance
- Results in infiltration loss
- Results in reduced oxygen exchange
- Results in loss of soil organics

The angle of repose:
- The natural angle at which a soil rests without compaction or manipulation
- The stable slope formed when soil is placed naturally
- Typically a 2:1 slope (two units horizontal for every one unit vertical) in most common soil conditions

Soil bearing capacity:
- The key soil characteristic that determines footing size
- The ability of soil to bear weight
- When designing a footing, the area must be large enough to distribute the load without exceeding bearing capacity

Soil plasticity:
- Achieved when enough moisture leaves the soil profile and the soil is no longer in a liquid state
- At this point the soil has some cohesive ability and enters its plastic state
- The plastic limit is the transition point from liquid to plastic state

Liquefaction:
- The condition where a soil acts like a liquid
- Typically occurs during earthquake conditions
- A risk in certain soil types that must be accounted for in foundation design in seismic zones
- In poor soil or liquefaction risk areas, only low-ground-pressure tracked equipment is appropriate

Maximum stockpile depth for native topsoil reserved for later planting use: 4 feet
- Deeper piles compact under their own weight
- Compaction removes oxygen and kills soil organisms and organic material

The USDA soil classification system is used in the United States to regulate agricultural practices and is the standard for evaluating soils for planting conditions.

Types of soil used in construction:
- Structural fill — approved solid structural fill material used under floor slabs and building foundations, for bridging over poor soil conditions
- Geotextile fabrics — used as separation layers between native soils and imported base materials, preventing migration of fines upward into the base course

TOPIC: RETAINING WALLS

Maximum height a landscape architect can design without a structural engineer: 3 feet

How retaining wall height is measured:
- From the bottom of the footing (including portions buried below finish grade) to the top of the wall
- NOT from finish grade to the top of the wall
- The buried portions of the footing count toward the total height for engineering purposes

The one design component common to ALL retaining wall types: drainage
- All walls must have a means of releasing water from the retained soil
- Prevents hydrostatic pressure buildup
- Hydrostatic pressure buildup is one of the most common causes of retaining wall failure

Drainage methods by wall type:
- Rigid concrete or masonry walls: weep holes
- Dry-stack or gabion walls: natural voids allow water to pass through
- Any rigid wall: perforated drain pipe installed at the base in a bed of gravel

Retaining wall types and characteristics:

Cantilever wall:
- Cast-in-place concrete
- Used for moderate to high walls
- Most costly type
- Greatest potential for height gain
- Offers options for finish and design pattern

Gravity wall (unit-stack):
- Materials such as dry-stack stone, keystone block, or crib and bin systems
- Low to moderate cost
- Quick to build and inexpensive
- No finish options

Gabion wall:
- Wire mesh baskets filled with crushed rock
- Easy to construct
- Relatively inexpensive
- Water passes through naturally — no separate drainage required
- Appropriate for non-aesthetic solutions where cost is a primary factor

Rockery wall:
- Large angular boulders stacked and fitted together
- Natural appearance
- Moderate cost

Soil-bioengineering wall:
- Reinforced embankment using geotextile materials, plantings, stone, or concrete surfacing

The batter of a retaining wall:
- The slight backward tilt built into the face of the wall
- A lean back into the slope
- Provides visual stability and helps resist the lateral load of the retained soil
- Both the exposed face and the back of a wall can have batter

The lateral load on a retaining wall:
- The force exerted by the soil bearing against the back of the wall
- Only soil that exceeds the angle of repose exerts pressure
- Depends on soil type and moisture content

Weep holes:
- Appropriate for any rigid form of wall construction
- A footing drain may also be used to relieve water pressure

TOPIC: CONCRETE DETAILS

Concrete strength fundamentals:
- Strength comes from the ratio of cement to aggregate
- More water in the mix weakens concrete by diluting the cement paste
- Too much water reduces compressive strength

Full strength timeline: 28 days after mixing and placement

The chemical process responsible for hardening: hydration
- Chemical bonding of cement particles with water molecules
- Continues until particles touch and form the hardened mass
- Concrete is still curing and gaining strength after 28 days but reaches design strength at 28 days

Common concrete testing methods:
- Slump test: most common on-site method, measures workability of the mix
- Proctor test: used to verify subgrade compaction before concrete is placed, establishes maximum dry density and optimum moisture content
- Cylinder test: measures compressive strength

Vibration during concrete pour:
- Used to consolidate the mix and fill all voids in forms
- Without vibration, air pockets form and weaken the finished structure

Most common placement method: cast-in-place (poured and pumped directly on site)

Concrete admixtures — materials added before or during mixing to modify properties:
- Retardant: slows the hydration process, giving more time to work the concrete — useful for long-distance transport or hot weather
- Accelerator: speeds up hydration
- Air entrainment: adds microscopic air bubbles that improve freeze-thaw resistance
- Reducer: reduces the amount of water needed
NOTE: A retardant is the admixture used to slow the setting of concrete.

Contraction joints (control joints):
- Control cracking as concrete expands and contracts with temperature and moisture changes
- Minimum depth: one-sixth the depth of the concrete slab
- A construction joint is a break that occurs between concrete pours or sections during installation

Welded wire mesh in a concrete slab:
- Increases slab tensile strength
- Helps control cracking
- Holds the slab together when cracks occur
- Does NOT increase compressive strength

Aggregate selection for roadway concrete:
- Primary criterion is rock hardness
- Rock hardness affects wearing ability under traffic
- Availability, aggregate size, and cost do not affect wearing surface performance as directly

Base aggregate layer for asphalt roadways:
- Should always extend beyond the finish pavement edge
- Provides drainage for the pavement surface
- Supports the outside edge of the pavement
- Discourages edge failure
- Satisfies municipal code requirements

Asphalt treated base (ATB):
- Used under the asphalt wearing course in wet or freezing conditions
- Can be worked in adverse weather
- Reduces subgrade damage from wet conditions
- Reduces erosion of control points during construction

Tack coat: used to bond two lifts of asphalt together
Primer: applied as the initial overlayment of the first course of asphalt on a prepared base
Binder: holds old and new coats of asphalt together
NOTE: These three terms are distinct and frequently tested.

The three aggregates in standard concrete mixes: sand, crushed stone, and gravel
NOTE: Cobblestone is NOT commonly used as aggregate in concrete.

Concrete full strength is reached at 28 days — this is a chemical process, not simply drying.

TOPIC: MASONRY DETAILS

Most common masonry materials in landscape construction:
- Brick
- Concrete
- Stone
NOTE: Clay, brick, concrete, and stone are the most common — listed in the practice materials as brick, concrete, and stone.

Mortar composition:
- Sand
- Cement
- Water
NOTE: Adding gravel or aggregate to mortar would make it concrete.

Mortar functions in masonry construction:
- Bonds masonry units together
- Compensates for irregularities in individual units
- Allows use of ties and reinforcing within units
- Provides color and texture enhancement

How to achieve a watertight masonry joint:
- Through a compressed, tooled joint
- Mason sets the block into the mortar
- Uses a rounded tool to compress the mortar into the joint
- Creates a solid watertight seal
NOTE: Compressed tooled joints are the correct answer — NOT protruding mortar joints or contraction joints.

Wall tie uses:
- Hold masonry units together
- Used to install veneered clay brick units by extending perpendicularly into the masonry unit
- In a masonry unit with brick veneer, steel rebar would be integral but would not hold the bricks — wall ties hold the bricks

Non-shrink grout:
- Used to install a railing into a sleeve embedded in a masonry wall
- Prevents water from entering the sleeve
- Secures the railing permanently

DBH stands for diameter breast height and is measured at 4.5 feet above grade.

TOPIC: WOOD AND DECK DETAILS

Deck structural hierarchy from top to bottom:
- Decking surface (transfers load to joists)
- Joists (carry the decking surface, attached to beams)
- Beams — the major load-carrying member (span between posts, carry the load of joists)
- Posts (support the beams)
- Footings (distribute the load to the soil)
- Railings (protect users from falls but are NOT structural load-carrying members)

When dead load is excessive on a deck:
- Deck posts will be CRUSHED — not bent or deflected
- Deflection occurs when joists are too far apart, causing the decking to sag

Wood member sizing reference: wood span tables
- Determine deck material sizes based on wood species, spans, and loads
- Sieve analysis is used for soil testing, NOT for sizing wood members
- ASTM standards govern manufacture and testing, NOT sizing
- Connector size tables are used for this situation but derive from wood span tables

Live loads for wooden decks: measured in pounds per square foot (psf)

Beam types:
- Continuous-span beam: spans more than two posts
- Cantilever beam: hangs over a post and ends before connecting to another post

Lumber terminology:
- Nominal-sized lumber: sized prior to kiln drying, shrinkage, and planing
- Unseasoned lumber (green lumber): freshly cut wood that has not been cured by kiln or air drying
- Kiln-dried lumber: moisture content is marked on the lumber adjacent to the kiln-dried stamp
- Finish-grade lumber: specified as having a kiln-dried appearance

Lumber is sold in North America in units of: board feet

Proper orientation for wood decking: BARK SIDE UP
- Allows cupping to occur away from the walking surface as wood expands and contracts
- Prevents a tripping hazard

Recommended nail length for wood decking:
- 2.5 times the thickness of the wood member being secured
- The nail must penetrate the full depth of the member and drive into the framing below

Nail length for common nail in decking installation:
- 2.5 times the thickness of the wood member being secured

Wood treatments for ground contact applications (acceptable):
- Ammoniacal copper arsenate (ACA)
- Chromated copper arsenate (CCA)
- Wolmanization
NOTE: These prevent rot and deter insects. Most treated wood has a life expectancy of 20 to 25 years.

Wood finishes for exposed exterior conditions NOT in ground contact (acceptable):
- Transparent stain
- Alkyd paint
- Polyurethane
NOTE: Acrylic latex paint is water-based and NOT ideal for fully exposed exterior applications.

TOPIC: FASTENER TYPES

Lag bolt (lag screw):
- Large fastener with hex head and coarse aggressive threading
- Used for heavy wood-to-wood or metal-to-wood connections
- Has wide aggressive thread pattern

Wood screw:
- Smaller fastener with aggressive threading
- Used for decking and light wood assemblies
- Used for small wood pieces and non-lateral sheer structural elements
- Small wood screws will shear in a lateral direction, so use is prohibited by code in some situations

Machine screw:
- Standard even threads throughout
- Used for metal-to-metal or metal-to-plastic connections
- Has standard thread pattern (not aggressive)

Anchor bolt:
- Cast in place in concrete
- Provides structural tie between a footing and the element above it
- Used for light standards, benches, walls, and deck ledgers attached to new concrete

Nail:
- The least expensive fastener type
- Used for general wood framing and decking
- Least expensive of all fastener types

Spike:
- A long galvanized metal nail
- Driven down through stacked timber layers to strengthen and connect them
- Used for landscape timber walls built in stacked layers (e.g., 4x6 timbers stacked four high)

Application-specific fastener selection:
- Wooden ledger attached to new concrete wall: anchor bolt embedded in new concrete
- Timber wall four pieces high using stacked timbers: spike
- Deck wood-to-wood connection requiring moisture sealing: construction adhesive in conjunction with bolting system
- Railing installed in sleeve in masonry wall: non-shrink grout

TOPIC: DRAINAGE DETAILS AND LOW IMPACT DEVELOPMENT

Subsurface drainage system components:
- Perforated pipe (surrounded by filter aggregate)
- Filter aggregate — typically washed gravel
- Geotextile fabric — wraps the aggregate to prevent fine soil particles from clogging the system
- The pipe is sloped toward an outlet

Geotextile fabrics in drainage and pavement applications:
- Separation layer between native soils and imported base materials
- Prevents migration of fines upward into the base course
- Used in applications involving poor or moisture-sensitive soils under impervious surfaces

Low Impact Development (LID):
- An alternative comprehensive stormwater management strategy
- Focused on reduction of environmental and economic impacts of site development
- Uses sustainable materials and concepts to maintain natural hydrologic cycles
- Especially appropriate for urban redevelopment projects and projects near environmentally sensitive areas

LID site design techniques:
- Rain gardens (bioretention systems)
- Porous pavements
NOTE: Stormwater discharge structures and detention facilities would NOT be LID techniques — they are traditional stormwater approaches that do not allow rainwater to recharge through soil contact.

Benefits of LID approach to stormwater management include:
- Enhanced landscape aesthetics and wildlife habitats
- Reduced construction costs
- Increased lot yield
- Reduced need for subsurface piping and detention pond infrastructure

Changes associated with traditional site development that LID corrects:
- Decreased soil porosity and infiltration capacity
- Increased areas of disturbance
- Increased areas of impervious surfaces
- Increased stormwater runoff velocity, volume, and duration
- Decreased areas of native vegetation

Rain garden (bioretention system):
- A planted depression that collects and stores stormwater from impervious surfaces
- Allows stormwater to infiltrate slowly into the ground
- Recharges groundwater
- Improves runoff water quality by filtering pollutants through the soil and plant system
- Three functions simultaneously: stores stormwater, recharges groundwater, and improves runoff water quality

Rain garden soil profile (per stormwater Best Management Practices):
- Should consist of compost-amended sandy soil
- Allows rapid infiltration
- Supports plant and microbial health
- Improves water quality through filtration
- Retains moisture to support vegetation between rain events

Plants most appropriate for rain gardens without supplemental irrigation:
- Native vegetation
- Acclimated to local climate and can handle both inundation and drought
NOTE: Evergreen shrubs, ornamental flowering shrubs, and shade trees require supplemental irrigation or cannot handle moisture fluctuations and are NOT the correct answer.

Why geotextile fabric is NOT appropriate in a rain garden:
- Becomes clogged with fine particulate matter over time
- Creates a thin impervious layer in the soil profile
- Reduces or eliminates the infiltration function
- Causes the rain garden to fail as a stormwater management system

Rain garden soil profile details (based on BMP cross-section):
- 6 inches flooding depth (max storm events only)
- 6 inches free board (minimum)
- Containment berm
- Non-woven geotextile fabric separation (between sandy soil and gravel)
- 18 inches of compost-amended sandy soil
- 4-inch layer of pea gravel
- Infiltration zone of washed rock
- 6-inch perforated pipe at base
- Undisturbed soil below

Best Management Practices (BMPs):
- Govern not only stormwater but also irrigation and on-site landscape management issues
- Through use of native plantings
- Reduced use of fertilizer, pesticides, and herbicides
- Reuse and proper amendment of on-site soil
- Main goal: recharge groundwater — promote on-site point-source water management and mitigation by allowing stormwater to infiltrate native subsoils as if the site was undeveloped

LEED — Leadership in Energy and Environmental Design:
- Program developed by the US Green Building Council (USGBC)
- Measures the performance efficiency and sustainability of buildings and their associated development
- Programs include site development and reuse of resources
- Placing important emphasis on reduction and reuse of water for irrigation

On-site soil recycling techniques that contribute to a healthy growing medium:
- Tilling soil
- Stockpiling and reusing on-site topsoil
- Amending the stockpiled soils with certified compost material
NOTE: Stockpiling soil in a large pile is NOT beneficial — it compacts. Covering material with plastic is NOT beneficial. Combining soil with on-site trenching spoils is NOT beneficial.

TOPIC: GREEN ROOF DETAILS

Green roof definition: a vegetated roof system installed on top of a waterproofed roof structure.

The seven layers of a green roof assembly from BOTTOM to TOP:
1. Structural roof deck
2. Waterproofing membrane (MOST CRITICAL LAYER — failure allows water and roots into building structure)
3. Root barrier (may be separate or combined with waterproofing membrane)
4. Drainage layer (lightweight aggregate, drainage mat, or modular drainage panels)
5. Filter fabric (separates drainage layer from growing medium, prevents fines migration)
6. Growing medium (specially engineered lightweight substrate — NOT native soil)
7. Vegetation

Growing medium characteristics (must be):
- Lightweight to minimize structural load
- Well-draining to prevent waterlogging
- Capable of supporting plant growth with minimal depth

Before designing a green roof:
- A structural engineer must verify the roof structure can handle the additional dead load

Green roof types:

Extensive green roof:
- Shallow growing medium — 2 to 6 inches deep
- Lightweight
- Minimal maintenance required
- Planted with low-growing drought-tolerant species such as sedums and native groundcovers

Intensive green roof:
- Deeper growing medium — 6 inches or more
- Can support wider range of plants including shrubs and small trees
- Requires more maintenance
- Imposes significantly greater structural load on the building

Benefits of green roofs:
- Stormwater management: absorb and slow the release of rainwater, reducing peak runoff
- Thermal insulation: growing medium and vegetation reduce building energy consumption
- Urban heat island reduction: vegetation cools the roof through evapotranspiration
- Extended roof membrane life: layers protect membrane from UV radiation and temperature extremes
- Habitat creation: provide habitat for birds and insects in urban environments

TOPIC: SUPPLEMENTAL PLANS — LIGHTING

Lighting plan content:
- Location and type of every luminaire (technical term for a light fixture)
- Mounting heights
- Photometric output in footcandles
- Circuit designations
- Conduit runs
- Panel location and schedule
- Control points such as timers, photocells, and manual switches

The three goals of landscape lighting:
- First: facilitate safety for users
- Second: improve the legibility of outdoor spaces and focal points at night
- Third: encourage use of a space during nighttime hours

The primary goal of pathway lighting: provide a safe travel route through the landscape

The most critical design factor in a recreational area lighting system: uniformity
- Non-uniform lighting creates shadows and blind spots
- Reduces safety and can cause injury
- Especially dangerous in sports and recreational spaces

Invalid criteria for landscape lighting design — this answer appears on the exam:
- Orienting fixtures so the source of the light is visible to the user
NOTE: The light source should NEVER be directly visible to users. Glare causes a blinding effect.

The unit used to measure illumination on a surface in the United States: footcandle
The international standard unit for illumination: lux
1 footcandle = approximately 10.76 lux

Minimum footcandle level for landscape lighting — per practice materials: 1 footcandle minimum

Installation qualifications by lighting type:
- Low-voltage lighting: can be installed by a licensed landscape contractor with limited permit requirements
- Architectural high-voltage lighting: requires electrical permit and must be installed by licensed electrical contractor
- Sports field lighting: requires electrical permit and must be installed by licensed electrical contractor

Proper grounding method for exterior lighting control panel:
- Install a ground wire with a welded connection to a grounding rod or plate
- Connect the panel to that ground
INCORRECT grounding methods:
- Connecting to a metal downspout
- Connecting to the metal conduit with incoming wires
NOTE: These do NOT adequately protect against lightning or power surges.

TOPIC: SUPPLEMENTAL PLANS — IRRIGATION

Irrigation plan content:
- Point of connection to the potable water source
- Backflow prevention device (double-check device)
- Main shutoff valve
- Zone control valves
- Mainline pipe routing
- Lateral lines from valves to heads
- Irrigation head locations with coverage radius and precipitation rate
- Irrigation controller location
- Rain or soil moisture sensors

Backflow prevention device (double-check device):
- Required when connecting to a potable water source
- Prevents irrigation water from flowing backward into the drinking water supply during a pressure drop
- Required by local codes in virtually every jurisdiction
- Without it, fertilizers, pesticides, and soil contaminants could contaminate drinking water

Maximum Applied Water Allowance (MAWA):
- The formula used to calculate total irrigation water allowed on a project when purchasing water rights
- Takes into account site area, reference evapotranspiration rate for the region, and types of plants being irrigated

Matching precipitation rates rule:
- When mixing irrigation heads of various radius sizes in the same zone, ALL heads must have matching precipitation rates
- This ensures uniform water distribution regardless of different coverage radii
- Mismatched precipitation rates lead to overwatering and underwatering in the same zone
- Manufacturers produce heads that vary in coverage radius but are calibrated to deliver the same precipitation rate

When to install irrigation sleeves:
- AFTER mass earthwork is complete
- BEFORE any permanent site construction — specifically before paving, concrete, or other hardscape
- Allows irrigation pipes and control wires to be threaded under hardscape later without demolishing finished work
- Missing this timing window requires costly demolition of finished hardscape

Cold climate irrigation design rule:
- All mainlines and lateral lines must be installed below the frost line depth
- Prevents pipes from freezing and bursting during winter

Equipment for irrigation installation by soil type:
- Colloidal or cohesive soil: chain trencher is most economical
- Sandy loam golf course (minimize site damage): turf tire wheel trencher
- Other soil conditions: various types of trenching equipment depending on scale and soil

Connecting irrigation to potable water — required device: backflow preventer

Irrigation control wire sizing:
- Valve wire must be sized to allow for the voltage drop at the end of each run of wire
- Voltage drop is determined using Ohm's law
- Ohm's law finds the voltage drop by multiplying the current (listed in the manufacturer's catalog for each valve) times the resistance for the type of wire used

TOPIC: SUPPLEMENTAL PLANS — PLAYGROUND

Playground design requirements:
- Fall zone (use zone): the area around and beneath play equipment that must be surfaced with impact-attenuating material
- Fall zone size is based on the height and type of the equipment
- Critical fall height: the maximum height from which a child can fall onto the surfacing material without sustaining a life-threatening head injury
- Surfacing must meet the critical fall height for the tallest piece of equipment

Impact-attenuating surfacing materials:
- Engineered wood fiber
- Loose fill rubber
- Shredded rubber mulch
- Pea gravel
- Sand
- Unitary rubber surfaces
- Synthetic turf surfaces

Governing standards for playground equipment:
- Consumer Product Safety Commission (CPSC) guidelines
- ASTM International standards
- These define acceptable equipment design, fall zone dimensions, surfacing requirements, and age-appropriate equipment configurations

Playground accessibility:
- Governed by the Americans with Disabilities Act (ADA)
- A certain percentage of ground-level and elevated play components must be accessible
- Accessible routes must connect accessible components throughout the playground

Pool barrier guidelines from the US Consumer Product Safety Commission:
- Pool top barrier must be at least 48 inches above grade, measured on the side facing away from the swimming pool
- Eliminate handholds and footholds
- Minimize the size of openings — all openings should be small enough that a 4-inch diameter sphere cannot pass through (based on head breadth and chest depth of a young child)
- Barrier bottom should not exceed 4-inch maximum clearance above grade, when measured on the side of the barrier facing away from the pool
- Barriers should be equipped with a gate or gates which restrict access to the pool
- A locking device should be included in the gate design
- Gates should open out from the pool and should be self-closing and self-latching

TOPIC: SUPPLEMENTAL PLANS — WAYFINDING

Wayfinding plan content:
- Location of all signs
- Type and mounting method of each sign
- Information content of each sign
- Orientation and direction of arrow elements
- Any lighting associated with signage

Principle of sign placement:
- Signs are placed at decision points where users must choose a direction
- Signs placed mid-route where there is no decision add clutter without value

Sign types in a wayfinding system:
- Identification signs: name a place or facility
- Directional signs: guide users toward destinations
- Informational signs: provide details about a location
- Regulatory signs: communicate rules and restrictions
- Interpretive signs: provide educational or cultural information about the site

ADA requirements for wayfinding:
- Sign heights are governed by ADA standards
- Mounting locations governed by ADA standards
- Character sizing for visually impaired users governed by ADA standards

TOPIC: QA/QC AND CODE COMPLIANCE

Quality Assurance (QA):
- Ongoing processes and procedures put in place to ensure quality throughout document production
- Includes consistent drawing standards, coordination protocols, and regular internal reviews
- Proactive — designed to prevent errors from being made in the first place

Quality Control (QC):
- Specific checking activities applied to finished products to catch errors before issuance
- The redline review is a classic quality control activity
- Reactive — catches what got through despite the QA process

Quality Control goals in construction document production:
- Preparing accurate and complete documents
- Designing a project that can be built on time and on budget
- Ensuring all regulatory requirements are met
NOTE: Reducing the number of client review meetings is NOT a quality control goal.

Value Engineering:
- A review of a project by a third-party team of experts
- Mission: improve performance, reliability, quality, safety, and life-cycle costs
- An independent third-party process
- NOT the same as internal quality control
- NOT the same as redesigning to reduce cost (though cost reduction may be an outcome)

Program Validation:
- The design team's independent review of the client's stated program and budget before design begins
- Identifies cost increases or unanticipated required tasks early
- Prevents investing design effort in a program that cannot be built within budget

Total Quality Management (TQM) — three components:
- Managing the quality of BOTH the design AND the design process (not just the final product)
- Upper management leading by example
- Assuming employees will do a good job if properly trained and given correct tools
NOTE: TQM does NOT focus quality efforts exclusively on final deliverables — it applies throughout the entire process.

Commissioning:
- The formal process of testing and verifying all installed systems at project completion
- Confirms they operate as designed
- Distinct from QC which applies during document production
- Commissioning happens after construction is complete

Code dimensions to memorize:
- OSHA minimum railing height for decks and platforms: 42 inches above adjacent deck surface
- Maximum spacing between railing members: 4 inches (based on size of a baby's head)
- Maximum planting height in site triangle at 35 mph road entrance: 30 inches

Site triangle definition:
- Area where all plantings must be kept below 30 inches
- Ensures drivers have clear visibility when entering or exiting a parking lot or driveway
- The dimensions of the site triangle depend on the speed of the adjacent road — faster road = larger triangle

Applicable codes and standards landscape architects commonly reference:
- Zoning regulations: each town's regulations concerning a structure's use, size, relationship to the site, and parking
- Building codes: published by the state, address how structures are to be constructed including door sizes and materials, window sizes and locations, structural lumber sizes, stair and hallway dimensions
- Uniform Building Code (ICBO)
- Uniform Plumbing Code
- Uniform Mechanical Code
- National Electric Code
- Council of American Building Officials (CABO) One and Two Family Dwelling Code
- International Energy Conservation
- All public developments must comply with accessibility laws and regulations to ensure access to public spaces for people with disabilities

TOPIC: PLANTING PLANS AND DETAILS

Planting plan components:
- Plant locations (shown at mature spread, not installed size)
- Plant tags or callouts
- Plant schedule (plant list)
- Planting notes

Why plants are shown at mature spread:
- Communicates long-term design intent
- Confirms that spacing is adequate for mature size
- Gives contractor and owner accurate picture of finished landscape

Plant schedule (plant list) contents:
- Symbol or abbreviation used on the plan
- Full botanical name
- Common name
- Total quantity required
- Specified size at installation
- Any special installation notes

Plant size specifications by type:
- Trees: specified by caliper — trunk diameter measured 6 inches above grade for trees up to 4-inch caliper, 12 inches above grade for larger trees
- Shrubs: height and spread or container size
- Groundcovers and perennials: container size (typically 1-gallon or 4-inch pots)

DBH (Diameter Breast Height): measured at 4.5 feet above grade, standard for specifying tree size

Trees failing to meet specified DBH should be rejected from the project.

Planting notes cover:
- Soil preparation requirements and amendments
- Planting hole dimensions
- Backfill mix specifications
- Fertilization requirements at planting
- Mulching requirements — type, depth, and placement
- Staking and guying — when required, hardware to use, how long to keep in place
- Warranty and establishment period terms

Correct planting hole preparation: SHALLOW AND WIDE
- Wide: allows roots to spread into loosened soil, promotes outward root development
- Shallow (matching root ball depth): prevents plant from settling too low, prevents suffocating the crown
- Deep narrow holes: compact soil below root ball, restrict oxygen, channel roots downward

Ball-and-burlap tree installation requirements:
- Set tree at correct elevation first
- Fully remove the twine from the pit
- Fully remove the wire basket from the pit
- Fully remove the burlap from the pit
- Do NOT fold any of these down into the pit — they leave air voids, impede root growth, and do not decompose quickly
- Only pruning: removal of dead and damaged branches ONLY
- No structural pruning, no crown reduction

Staking requirements and timeline:
- Staking is only appropriate when severe weather conditions exist OR when tree size does not allow self-support
- Unnecessary staking impedes trunk strength development
- When used: loosen stakes after the FIRST growing season
- When used: REMOVE stakes entirely after the SECOND growing season
- Leaving stakes and guy wires in place indefinitely can girdle the tree

Mulch requirements:
- Moderates soil temperature
- Retains soil moisture
- Suppresses weed growth
- MUST NEVER contact the trunk or stem of a plant
- Burying the trunk in mulch causes rot and disease (called volcano mulching)
- Pull back from the trunk several inches
- Spread evenly over the full root zone

Most important factor for long-term plant health at installation:
- The condition of the plants upon arrival at the planting site
- Inspect immediately for dryness, physical damage, and unhealed wounds in the bark

Best planting season: FALL
- Allows root systems to establish before summer heat or drought
- Root development focus before encountering seasonal stress

Second best planting season: Spring
- But spring focuses energy on above-ground growth rather than root development
- Leaves the root system less established going into first summer

Worst planting seasons: Summer and Winter (most stressful, avoid when possible)

USDA plant hardiness zones:
- Based on average annual minimum winter temperature
- General indication of which plant species will survive in a given location
- Not sufficient alone for selecting specific cultivars
- Cultivar hardiness also depends on where the plant was grown (nursery location matters)
- Always verify suitability with a local reputable nursery

Organization that sets standards for plant material health, size, shape, and vigor:
- ANLA — American Nursery and Landscape Association
NOTE: NOT ANSI (general safety standards), NOT ASTM (testing and materials), NOT CSI (specifications organization)

Stakes and guy wires for trees should remain in place for:
- Loosened after one growing season
- Removed entirely after two growing seasons

Planting season for most landscape materials — best season and reason:
- Fall is best because plants can establish root systems during mild fall and winter temperatures before facing summer heat or drought
