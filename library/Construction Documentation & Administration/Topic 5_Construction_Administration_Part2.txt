TOPIC: SITE OBSERVATIONS VS. INSPECTION — THE CRITICAL DISTINCTION

This is one of the most heavily tested concepts in all of construction administration.

What the landscape architect does NOT do during construction:
- Does NOT supervise the contractor
- Does NOT inspect the contractor's work
- Does NOT control the contractor's means and methods of construction
- Is NOT responsible for the safety of the contractor's workers on the job site
- Does NOT tell the contractor's workers how to do their jobs
- Does NOT direct the contractor's labor

What the landscape architect DOES do:
- OBSERVES — conducts periodic site observations
- Visits the site at key milestones or on a regular schedule
- Assesses whether the work appears to be proceeding in general conformance with the contract documents
- Is NOT on site every day watching every action
- Visits, observes, documents, and reports

Why the distinction between observation and inspection matters legally:
- If a contract describes the landscape architect as INSPECTING or SUPERVISING, it implies they are controlling the work and are responsible for its correctness
- If something goes wrong and the contract says the landscape architect was inspecting or supervising, they may face liability for a problem that was actually the contractor's fault
- Words have legal meaning in construction contracts
- The correct language is OBSERVATION or CONSTRUCTION OBSERVATION
- The words SUPERVISION, INSPECTION, and DIRECTION should be avoided when describing the landscape architect's construction phase role

What the landscape architect looks for during a site observation:
- Are the right materials being used?
- Is work occurring in the right locations per the drawings?
- Are dimensions and elevations consistent with the construction documents?
- Are there any visible deficiencies — cracked concrete, improperly graded surfaces, damaged plants, missing elements?
- Are there any conditions in the field that conflict with the contract documents and need to be resolved through an RFI or bulletin?
- Is work proceeding in general conformance with the contract documents?

When a deficiency is identified during a site observation:
- It MUST be communicated to the contractor IN WRITING
- A verbal comment to the contractor's superintendent is not sufficient documentation
- The deficiency must be noted in the field report
- If the contractor fails to correct a noted deficiency, the written field report is the evidence

Critical legal principle: If the landscape architect observes a deficiency and fails to document it or bring it to the contractor's attention, this can be interpreted as TACIT ACCEPTANCE of the deficiency.
- Silence in the face of known defects is not approval — but it creates ambiguity
- That ambiguity can work against the owner and design team in subsequent disputes
- Document EVERYTHING — every observation, every deficiency, every conversation about quality

The landscape architect's primary role during construction observation:
- Ensure that the work is proceeding in general conformance with the intent of the contract documents
- Address change orders
- Ensure specified means and methods are followed
- NOT to ensure that contract labor laws are followed — that is the contractor's responsibility

Construction observation techniques can be performed:
- By an individual or firm as a result of on-site visits
- OR by a full-time onsite project representative

Observation techniques include:
- Observing the installation of a project to assure compliance with the construction documents
- Preparing change orders
- Resolving site and design issues
- Reviewing project submittals and making recommendations for approval or rejection
- Preparing project documentation: daily reports, photography, field measurements, construction notification memos, bi-monthly summary reports, and meeting memos
- Monitoring all independent testing with respect to material, compaction, and utilities to determine compliance with all applicable regulations
- Preparing the punch list from outstanding work items discussed and filed confirmed
- Informing the client if construction practices are contrary to the norm
- Notifying the client of defects and deficiencies in the work of the contractor
- Performing final review of the project

TOPIC: FIELD REPORTS

A field report (also called site observation report or construction observation report):
- Written record of each site observation visit
- NOT optional — every site visit must result in a field report
- Should be issued promptly after each visit — ideally within 24 to 48 hours
- A field report written two weeks after the visit from memory is far less reliable and credible

A complete field report must contain:
- Date of the visit
- Weather conditions — temperature, precipitation, and general site conditions
- Names of ALL persons present — landscape architect, contractor's representatives, owner's representatives, any subcontractors encountered
- Description of the work observed — what was being installed, what stage each element was at, apparent quality and conformance
- List of any deficiencies observed — described with enough specificity to identify exactly what and where the problem is
- Any verbal instructions given to the contractor during the visit — confirmed in writing through the field report or follow-up letter
- Open items requiring follow-up — questions needing answers, items needing resubmittal, deficiencies needing correction
- References to photographs taken during the visit

Purposes field reports serve simultaneously:
- Keep the owner informed about progress and quality of the work
- Create a running written record of the project as it is built
- Document the sequence of construction and conditions at each stage
- Essential evidence if a dispute arises later — if a contractor claims they were never notified about a deficiency, the field report is the evidence
- Demonstrate the landscape architect's professional diligence — that they were performing their contractual obligations

A daily construction report is a written record of the day's activities:
- Weather conditions
- Work accomplished
- Number of personnel for each trade involved
- Equipment involved
- Materials received
- Visitors or inspectors present
- Problems encountered

TOPIC: BONDS — ALL TYPES

IMPORTANT: Bonds are guarantees of performance — they are NOT insurance.

Every bond involves three parties:
- The PRINCIPAL: the party with the obligation to perform — typically the contractor
- The OBLIGEE: the party to whom the obligation is owed — typically the owner
- The SURETY: the bonding company that guarantees the principal's performance to the obligee; if the principal fails, the surety steps in

Bid bond:
- Submitted with the contractor's bid during competitive bidding
- Guarantees that if the contractor is the successful bidder, they WILL execute the contract and provide all required performance and payment bonds
- Protects the owner from the situation where the low bidder refuses to sign the contract
- Without a bid bond: owner has no recourse and must rebid the project
- With a bid bond: owner can recover the difference between the defaulting bidder's price and the next lowest bid
- Typically expressed as a percentage of the bid amount — commonly 5 or 10 percent
- May also be provided as a certified check or cashier's check on smaller projects

Performance bond:
- Provided by the contractor AFTER contract award, as a condition of executing the contract
- Guarantees the faithful performance of the contractor's obligations under the construction contract
- Up to the face value of the bond
- Protects the owner against contractor default
- If contractor abandons project, goes bankrupt, fails to perform, or is terminated for cause: owner can make a claim against the performance bond
- Surety's three options upon contractor default:
  1. Find and pay a completion contractor to finish the work
  2. Take over and complete the work itself
  3. Pay the owner the cost of completion up to the bond amount
- Performance bonds are typically written for 100 percent of the contract value
- Continues in effect through the guarantee period after substantial completion
- If contractor fails to honor warranty obligations during guarantee period, owner may have recourse against the performance bond

Payment bond:
- Also provided by the contractor as a condition of executing the contract
- Guarantees that the contractor will pay ALL subcontractors, suppliers, and laborers
- Addresses the mechanics lien risk for the owner

Mechanics lien explained:
- A legal claim that an unpaid subcontractor or supplier can file against the OWNER'S PROPERTY to secure payment
- The scenario: owner pays general contractor in full; general contractor fails to pay subcontractors; subcontractors file liens against the owner's property
- The owner has already paid but is now facing liens from parties they never contracted with
- With a payment bond: the unpaid subcontractors and suppliers can make claims against the BOND rather than against the owner's property
- The payment bond removes the owner's exposure to mechanics liens
- Payment bonds protect two parties: the owner (from mechanics liens) and the subcontractors/suppliers (from an unpaying contractor)

Surety bond general concept:
- A three-party guarantee arrangement
- Principal (contractor) has an obligation; obligee (owner) is owed the obligation; surety (bonding company) guarantees the obligation
- If the principal fails to perform, the surety steps in
- Bonds are NOT insurance — they are guarantees of performance

Liquidated damages:
- A specific dollar amount agreed upon in ADVANCE in the construction contract
- Charged against the contractor for each CALENDAR DAY that project completion is delayed beyond the contract completion date
- NOT a penalty — a pre-agreed estimate of the owner's actual costs of late completion
- The daily rate must be reasonably tied to the owner's actual anticipated costs of delay

Why liquidated damages exist:
- Proving actual cost of delay in court is difficult, expensive, and time-consuming
- Instead, parties agree at the outset on a daily rate representing a reasonable estimate of the owner's costs
- May include: cost of extended construction management, delayed opening and lost revenue, ongoing financing costs, and other carrying costs

For liquidated damages to be legally enforceable:
- Must be a GENUINE PRE-ESTIMATE of anticipated damages
- NOT an unreasonably high penalty designed to punish the contractor
- Courts will NOT enforce a liquidated damages clause that functions as a penalty rather than compensation

When liquidated damages stop accruing:
- Typically at SUBSTANTIAL COMPLETION — when the owner can occupy and use the facility
- After substantial completion, the owner has essentially received the use of the project

Interaction with time extensions:
- If the contractor is entitled to a time extension, the extension pushes back the contract completion date
- This pushes back the point at which liquidated damages begin to accrue

Retainage (also covered in File 4 — repeated here for completeness):
- A percentage of each progress payment — typically 5 to 10 percent — withheld throughout construction
- In the context of bonds and legal documents: Retainage is a percentage of the total bid which a contractor or subcontractor pays upon submitting a bid for work — it is considered insurance and a good faith tool
- The money is held in escrow and paid back with interest when the contractor has fulfilled obligations under the terms of the agreement

TOPIC: INSURANCE TYPES IN CONSTRUCTION

Insurance protects against risk — the insured pays premiums, and in exchange the insurer pays for covered losses.
DISTINCTION: Insurance protects against risk. Bonds guarantee performance of a specific obligation.

General liability insurance:
- Carried by the CONTRACTOR
- Protects against claims for bodily injury and property damage arising from the contractor's operations
- If a member of the public is injured on or adjacent to the construction site due to contractor's negligence, general liability covers the claim
- Contract documents specify minimum coverage limits

Workers compensation insurance:
- Required by law in virtually every state
- Provides medical benefits and wage replacement to workers injured on the job regardless of fault
- The contractor's responsibility
- The landscape architect should verify this coverage through the contractor's certificate of insurance before construction begins

Builder's risk insurance (course of construction insurance):
- Covers the PROJECT ITSELF against physical loss during the construction period
- Covered events: fire, theft, vandalism, wind, and other covered perils
- Insures the physical structure as it is being built
- Typically carried by the owner or the contractor — the contract should specify who is responsible

Professional liability insurance (errors and omissions — EO insurance):
- Carried by the LANDSCAPE ARCHITECT and other design professionals
- Covers claims arising from professional negligence — errors or omissions in the design work that cause damages
- Is CLAIMS-MADE — covers claims FILED during the policy period, not claims arising from events during the policy period
- The landscape architect must maintain coverage CONTINUOUSLY, even after a project is complete, to be protected against claims that arise years later
- Courts apply several types of standards of care to determine whether a design professional has carried out their responsibilities

Certificate of insurance:
- The landscape architect should request and review certificates of insurance from the contractor before authorizing the Notice to Proceed
- Certificate should confirm: all required insurance types are in place, coverage limits meet contract requirements, owner and landscape architect are named as additional insureds where required

Standards courts use to evaluate professional liability:
- Specifications and performance guidelines contained in the contract
- Application of a process-based standard focusing on the reasonableness of the process used to accomplish the client's objectives
- Examination of the suitability of the finished product for its intended uses based on theory of implied or explicit warranty

TOPIC: CONSTRUCTION DELAYS AND THEIR CONSEQUENCES

Three fundamental categories of construction delay:

1. OWNER-CAUSED DELAYS:
Definition: Delays that result directly from the owner's actions or inactions.

Examples of owner-caused delays:
- Owner fails to provide access to the site when promised
- Owner fails to make a timely decision about a design change
- Owner fails to return submittals or approve shop drawings within the required timeframe
- Owner orders a work stoppage
- Landscape architect (acting as owner's agent) fails to respond to RFIs in a timely manner

Consequences of owner-caused delays:
- Contractor is entitled to BOTH a contract duration extension AND additional compensation for delay costs
- Contractor gets MORE TIME and MORE MONEY
- Reasoning: if the contractor's work is delayed by actions of the owner, the contractor should not bear the financial cost

Delay costs the contractor may recover for owner-caused delays:
- Extended general conditions costs (job site trailer, temporary utilities, site supervision for extended duration)
- Idle equipment costs for equipment that cannot be used during the delay period
- Escalation costs if material prices have increased during the delay period
- Extended home office overhead costs

2. EXCUSABLE DELAYS (FORCE MAJEURE DELAYS):
Definition: Delays caused by events beyond the control of EITHER the owner OR the contractor.

Common examples of excusable delays:
- Acts of God such as earthquakes, floods, and hurricanes
- Unusually severe weather that goes beyond what could reasonably be anticipated for the region and season
- Labor strikes by unions not employed on the project
- Acts of government such as an embargo or unusual permit delays beyond the contractor's control
- Epidemics or other extraordinary circumstances

Consequences of excusable delays:
- Contractor is entitled to a TIME EXTENSION ONLY
- Contractor does NOT receive additional compensation for delay costs
- Reasoning: neither party caused the delay, so neither should bear the financial consequences
- The owner gets the extra time reflected in a longer schedule
- The contractor does not incur liquidated damages for the extended duration

Important nuance — what qualifies as excusable weather:
- NOT every bad weather day qualifies
- Ordinary seasonal rain, wind, and cold are foreseeable — the contractor is expected to account for these in their schedule
- Only weather that is TRULY UNUSUAL — significantly beyond historical norms for that time and place — qualifies
- The contractor must typically document weather events and compare them to historical averages

3. CONTRACTOR-CAUSED DELAYS:
Definition: Delays resulting from the contractor's own actions, inactions, or failures.

Examples of contractor-caused delays:
- Failure to order materials or equipment in time
- Inadequate staffing for the scope of work
- Poor coordination between subcontractors
- Failure to submit required submittals on schedule
- Subcontractor performance failures
- Underestimating the complexity or duration of the work

Consequences of contractor-caused delays:
- Contractor is entitled to NEITHER a time extension NOR additional compensation
- The contractor is expected to manage their own work
- If contractor-caused delays push the project past the contract completion date, the owner may be entitled to assess LIQUIDATED DAMAGES for each day of delay

Concurrent delays:
- Delays caused simultaneously by both the owner and the contractor, or by the contractor and an excusable event
- Among the most legally complex situations in construction contract administration
- Makes it very difficult to apportion responsibility
- The contractor may be entitled to a time extension for the owner-caused portion
- May not be entitled to full delay costs if the contractor's own actions also contributed
- Courts and arbitrators handle concurrent delay claims differently by jurisdiction and contract language
- Best prevention: careful contemporaneous documentation of delay causes by both parties

The contractor's duty to mitigate delay:
- Even when a delay is owner-caused or excusable, the contractor has a duty to take REASONABLE STEPS to minimize the delay's impact
- Cannot simply stop all work when a delay occurs
- Must shift work to unaffected areas
- Must resequence activities where possible
- Must do everything reasonable to avoid amplifying the delay

NOTICE OF DELAY requirement:
- Most construction contracts require the contractor to provide written notice of a delay within a specified period — often within a few days of when the delay first occurs or becomes known
- If the contractor fails to provide timely notice, they may WAIVE their right to a time extension or additional compensation
- Even if the delay was legitimately the owner's fault
- The notice requirement exists so the owner has an opportunity to investigate and potentially mitigate the delay

TOPIC: CONSTRUCTION ACCELERATION

Three distinct types — know all three and be able to distinguish them:

Actual acceleration:
- An increase in the pace of construction that results from a specific WRITTEN DIRECTIVE from the owner
- The owner explicitly tells the contractor to speed up
- Typically results in additional cost to the contractor
- Compensated through a change order

Constructive acceleration:
- An increase in the pace of construction NOT the result of an explicit directive — happens by inference or implication
- The most common scenario: contractor submits a legitimate request for a time extension, owner denies the request; without a time extension, contractor is forced to speed up to meet the original deadline even though they should have been entitled to more time
- The acceleration was CONSTRUCTIVE — forced on the contractor without a direct order
- Can still entitle the contractor to additional compensation even though the owner never explicitly directed them to accelerate

For a constructive acceleration claim to succeed, the contractor must typically demonstrate:
- A valid excusable delay existed
- The owner refused to extend the contract time
- The contractor had to accelerate to avoid being in default

Fast-tracking:
- A project delivery and schedule strategy — NOT a type of construction acceleration
- Refers to OVERLAPPING design and construction phases to compress the overall project schedule
- Example: contractor begins foundation work while design of upper floors is still ongoing
- NOT the same as speeding up the pace of construction — it is about overlapping phases

TOPIC: PROJECT CLOSEOUT — COMPLETE SEQUENCE

The closeout process sequence:
1. Contractor believes work is substantially complete and submits written notice to landscape architect
2. Landscape architect conducts substantial completion inspection with contractor and owner
3. Substantial completion is determined and punch list is prepared
4. Contractor works through punch list items
5. Contractor notifies landscape architect that all punch list items are addressed
6. Landscape architect conducts final inspection
7. Final completion is certified if all punch list items are satisfactorily resolved
8. Close-out documents submitted
9. Final lien waiver provided
10. Remaining retainage released
11. Final payment made

Substantial completion:
- The status of a project when it is sufficiently complete to be occupied and used by the owner for its intended purpose
- Even if some minor work remains to be done
- Does NOT mean everything is done — means the project is USABLE

What happens at substantial completion:
- The owner can begin occupying and using the project
- The guarantee period BEGINS — the clock starts on contractor's warranty obligations
- Liquidated damages STOP accruing — owner can no longer assess liquidated damages after substantial completion
- A portion of retainage is typically released — often half — with remainder held until final completion
- The owner assumes responsibility for insurance and utilities for occupied portions
- A Notice of Substantial Completion is issued

A Notice of Partial Completion:
- Issued when the owner may desire to occupy a PORTION of a project
- Local building officials conduct an inspection to determine if that portion meets all building codes and is safe to be occupied

A Notice of Substantial Completion:
- Issued by the local building officials when 95 percent of the construction is complete

A Notice of Completion/Certificate of Occupancy:
- Requested by the contractor/owner when the building is 100 percent complete
- The project must pass a final inspection by local building officials

The punch list:
- A written list of all remaining work items requiring completion or correction before final completion
- Prepared during the substantial completion inspection
- The landscape architect and owner walk the project together and note every item requiring attention
- Each item must be SPECIFIC and UNAMBIGUOUS — not "fix the planting" but "replace the dead Japanese maple at the northeast corner of the main entry plaza, approximately eight feet tall"

Common punch list items in landscape architecture:
- Plants that have died or are in poor health and need replacement
- Missing plants that were specified but not installed
- Finish defects in paving, walls, or structures that need repair
- Incomplete or incorrect signage installation
- Irrigation system adjustments needed for proper coverage
- Missing or damaged site furnishings
- Site cleanup items such as debris removal and grade repairs

Final inspection:
- Conducted by the landscape architect when the contractor believes all punch list items have been addressed
- A walkthrough of the entire project specifically to verify every punch list item has been satisfactorily resolved
- If all items are complete: the landscape architect certifies FINAL COMPLETION
- If items are not satisfactorily addressed: the landscape architect should NOT certify final completion; contractor must continue working

Final completion:
- The status when ALL work including ALL punch list items has been completed in full conformance with the contract documents
- This is the true end of the contractor's obligations under the construction contract

What happens at final completion:
- The remaining retainage (the half not released at substantial completion) is released
- The final payment application is processed and paid
- The contractor provides a final lien waiver
- All required close-out documents are submitted
- The contractor's obligation to perform work under the contract is complete

Close-out documents the contractor must submit:
1. As-built drawings (record drawings): show the project as actually constructed, incorporating all field changes made during construction
   - Essential for future maintenance, repairs, and modifications
   - The definitive record of what was built, where everything is located, and how it was constructed
   - Failure to submit can be grounds for withholding final payment
   - Different from shop drawings (submitted before fabrication) — as-builts are submitted after construction

2. Operation and maintenance manuals (O&M manuals):
   - Required for any systems installed — irrigation controllers, lighting panels, specialty equipment
   - Include manufacturer's documentation, warranty information, recommended maintenance schedules, and troubleshooting guides
   - Must be organized and submitted in a format the owner can actually use

3. Warranties and guarantees:
   - All manufacturer warranties for installed products transferred to the owner
   - Contractor's own workmanship warranty confirmation
   - Landscape architect compiles a warranty log listing every warranty, the covered product, duration, and contact information for claims

4. Final lien waiver:
   - Confirms that all subcontractors, suppliers, and laborers have been paid in full
   - Confirms no mechanics liens are outstanding against the owner's property
   - The owner's assurance that paying the final retainage will not expose them to unpaid claims from third parties

The guarantee period (warranty period):
- Typically ONE YEAR from substantial completion (may vary by contract)
- During this period: contractor must return to the site and correct any defects or failures that become apparent
- At no additional cost to the owner
- If the owner discovers a defect: they notify the contractor in writing; contractor investigates and corrects
- The guarantee period is NOT the same as manufacturer warranties — manufacturer warranties run independently on their own terms
- The landscape architect assists during the guarantee period: evaluating defects, communicating claims to contractor, verifying corrections, tracking open items

Commissioning:
- The formal process of testing and verifying all equipment and systems at project completion
- Confirms they operate as designed
- Involves running systems under actual operating conditions and verifying performance against specifications
- Examples: running irrigation system through all zones to verify coverage and flow, testing lighting for proper photometric performance
- Distinct from QC which applies during document production
- Commissioning happens AFTER construction is complete as part of closeout

TOPIC: CONSTRUCTION PROJECT MANAGEMENT — ROLES AND RESPONSIBILITIES

The project manager (PM):
- Leads the project team
- Responsible for scope, schedule, and budget — the three legs of the project management triangle
- When any of these changes, it typically affects the other two
- Primary point of contact for the client
- Responsible for: defining scope, planning work, directing work, coordinating with client, monitoring progress, and learning from outcomes

The project controller:
- Responsible for managing project finances
- Works with the PM to keep the project financially on track
- Tracks actual costs against budgeted costs
- Monitors the rate at which budget is being consumed vs. work being produced
- Identifies when a project is at risk of going over budget
- Helps develop and implement budget recovery strategies
- DISTINCT FROM the construction administrator — they are separate roles

The construction administrator:
- The landscape architect or firm representative who manages the construction administration phase
- Period from contract execution through project closeout
- Conducts site observations
- Reviews and responds to RFIs
- Processes submittals
- Administers the change order process
- Certifies pay applications
- Manages the closeout process
- Primary communications link between the design team and the contractor during construction
- Does NOT administer or direct the contractor's labor
- Does NOT tell the contractor's workers how to do their jobs

Coordination with other disciplines:
- Most landscape architecture projects involve coordination with civil engineers, structural engineers, mechanical engineers, electrical engineers, and architects
- During construction administration, the landscape architect must coordinate to resolve conflicts between disciplines
- Construction documents must coordinate with civil grading and utility plans, structural wall and foundation details, electrical site lighting, and other discipline work
- Conflicts discovered during construction must be resolved promptly through RFI responses or bulletins

OAC meetings (owner-architect-contractor meetings):
- Regular coordination meetings held during construction
- Typically monthly or biweekly
- Attendees: owner, landscape architect, and contractor
- Purpose: review schedule progress, address open RFIs and submittals, discuss upcoming work, identify and resolve conflicts before they delay construction
- The landscape architect typically prepares the meeting agenda AND the meeting minutes

Meetings during construction:
- Job Start Meeting: occurs before starting work; introduces contractor personnel to other parties; reviews all requests for facilities; permits designers to review and interpret project documents
- Facility Review: walk-through of all work areas with owner's representative; contractor describes specific work methods and proposed schedules; allows identification of areas of concern, desired installation, and time lines; review of important procedural and safety precautions
- Weekly Project Meetings: designed to review the status of current and planned activities, the schedule, and to conduct other business associated with the project
- Site Safety Meetings: attended by subcontractors or representation at the general contractor's weekly on-site safety meeting

TOPIC: PROFESSIONAL LIABILITY IN CONSTRUCTION ADMINISTRATION

The landscape architect is liable for:
- Errors and omissions in the construction documents (mistakes in drawings or specifications that cause the contractor to build something incorrectly)
- Failures in construction administration (certifying payment for incomplete work, approving non-conforming submittals, failing to identify significant deficiencies during site observations)

The landscape architect is generally NOT liable for:
- The contractor's means and methods of construction
- The contractor's safety practices on the job site
- Dimensional errors in fabricated work not caught in submittal review — that remains the contractor's responsibility
- Problems caused by the contractor's own negligence

Why language in contracts and field reports matters:
- Using words like "observation" instead of "inspection" accurately describes the role as assessment, not control
- "Inspection" or "supervision" imply control over the contractor's work and can create liability
- This is professional liability management, not just semantics

The landscape architect's standard of care:
- Courts have stated that design professionals have the obligation to exercise a reasonable degree of care, skill, and ability
- This standard means any work done must be at least as good as the work of a competent person with average skills and experience in that type of work
- Expert testimony is usually needed to establish this standard of care in legal proceedings
- A design professional can limit liability to a client largely through the contract

TOPIC: SUSTAINABLE PRACTICES IN CONSTRUCTION ADMINISTRATION

Landscape maintenance materials:
- Edging: materials used to separate landscape areas from other areas and from other uses on the property; forms include treated wood, stone, concrete, or plastic
- Weed barrier: woven fabric that inhibits growth of weeds in landscape areas
- Mulch materials: act to condition the soil and inhibit growth of weeds
- Ground cover materials: variety of materials used for covering fabric weed barrier; also a variety of mulch materials and rock materials
- Root guard protection materials
- Stepping stones and pavers

Basic landscape maintenance practices:
- Aeration: improving the gas and air exchange capabilities of soil being utilized for growing plant material
- Fertilization: supplemental application of nutrients required in the soil for healthy and sustained plant growth
- Mowing operations
- Mulching: placement of organic material over a plant's root zone
- Pest and disease control: minimizing or eliminating damaging insects, animals, and pathogens
- Pruning: selective removal of foliage or branches from plant material
- Soil amendments: any materials added to the soil to improve or maintain its texture, pH, or friability to encourage healthy plant growth
- Trimming: removal of excess or unwanted turfgrass or similar plant material
- Weed control: controlling or eliminating unwanted plant material

Landscape maintenance techniques that should follow appropriate recommendations include:
- Managing irrigation and runoff
- Watering according to plant needs
- Fertilizing according to a soil analysis
- Pruning to shape a plant, to allow air circulation, or to remove dead, diseased, or damaged tissue
- Aeration of mulched beds and turf areas
- Mowing of lawn areas
- Storing and disposing of gardening wastes properly
- Mulching or other erosion control measures on exposed soils
- Managing pesticide and herbicide use properly
- Incorporating pest management techniques where appropriate
