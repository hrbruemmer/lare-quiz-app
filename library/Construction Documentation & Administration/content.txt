LARE SECTION 3 — PROJECT AND CONSTRUCTION ADMINISTRATION
Complete Reference Database
Sources: Practice Exam, Erik Sweet Section D Practice Problems, Jimmie Hinze Construction
Contracts, Mometrix LARE Flashcard Study System
TOPIC 1: PROJECT MANAGEMENT FUNDAMENTALS
The project manager is the person who leads the project team and is responsible for defining
the scope of work, planning and directing design work, coordinating with the client, and
monitoring progress. The project manager is the single point of accountability for the success
of a project.
The six main activities of a project manager are: defining the scope of work, planning the
design work, directing the design work, coordinating with the client, monitoring progress
against schedule and budget, and learning from project outcomes to improve future
performance.
The five phases of project management in correct order are: Start, Planning, Design,
Production, and Closeout. These are not the same as traditional design phase names.
The five basic functions of management are: planning, organizing, commanding, coordinating,
and controlling. Eliminating risk is not one of the five functions.
Management by Objectives (MBO) is a method by which projects are managed by measuring
progress toward achieving stated objectives rather than by tracking activities or tasks. The
focus is on results and outcomes, not on what people are doing.
The concept that timeliness is more important than perfection is expressed by the phrase: the
perfect is the enemy of the good. Design decisions must be made when the schedule requires
them, not postponed while waiting for all information to be available.
Synergy, described by Mary Follett in 1918, is the process by which the effect of a group is
more than the sum of its parts. A well-functioning collaborative team produces better work
than the same individuals working independently.
A charrette is an intensive collaborative design process conducted on site over multiple days
with a multidisciplinary team. It is characterized by three features: it is conducted on site and
lasts multiple days; teams are multidisciplinary so integrated solutions are produced; and
each session is followed by a feedback discussion with stakeholders.
TOPIC 2: ORGANIZATIONAL STRUCTURES FOR DESIGN FIRMS
The four basic organizational structures for design firms are: sole proprietorship, design
studio, matrix organization, and ad hoc project team.
A sole proprietorship is a single individual who owns and operates the business. The sole
proprietor is personally liable for all business obligations with no legal separation between the
individual and the firm.
A design studio is a small to medium-sized single-discipline firm headed by a principal leading
a team of designers and support staff. Studios often have fluid organizational structures with
informal reporting relationships.
A matrix organization is found in medium to large multi-discipline firms. Employees report
along two axes. Vertical reporting is discipline-specific — department heads are responsible
for technical quality within their specialty across all projects. Horizontal reporting is
project-specific — project managers are responsible for overall project success across all
disciplines and phases.
Siloing is the tendency of working groups within a large organization to lose sight of the
concerns of other groups and focus only on their own area. In design firms it causes
coordination failures and conflicts in construction documents. The matrix organization
combats siloing through horizontal reporting.
An ad hoc project team is assembled specifically for a single project around a single project
manager and disbands when the project is complete. Members are drawn from within the firm
or from outside consultants based on project needs.
The four requirements for an empowered team are: a clear scope of work and clearly defined
roles for each member; delegation of technical decisions to the right team members; team
buy-in to the work plan; and appropriate resources and authority. The project manager does
NOT relinquish management of scope, budget, and schedule in an empowered team.
Offshoring is the practice of using service providers located outside of North America to
perform production work such as drafting or CAD work at a lower cost or faster turnaround. It
is distinct from domestic outsourcing.
TOPIC 3: PROJECT WORK PLANNING — TASKS, SUBTASKS, AND DELIVERABLES
A task is an essential activity or increment of work that can be defined and used to build a
work breakdown schedule. Every task has three defining elements: an objective — what the
task is meant to accomplish; a duration — the calendar time budgeted to complete the task;
and a level of effort — the number of labor hours budgeted to accomplish the objective.
A subtask is a detailed action that will lead to the completion of a task. Subtasks break a task
down into its component steps.
A deliverable is a tangible or intangible product of work that must be provided to someone
else. Deliverables are what the project produces. Tasks are performed to produce
deliverables.
Level of effort is the number of labor hours budgeted to accomplish an objective, measurable
in hours or labor dollars. It differs from duration — duration is calendar time, level of effort is
actual work time.
The Work Breakdown Structure (WBS) is a project planning tool that divides a project into
hierarchical tasks, identifying broad tasks first and breaking them down to the level of detail
needed to control the work. It is the foundation on which the project schedule and budget are
built.
The six objectives of a Project Work Plan are: define project objectives and identify the project
team; establish a project schedule; establish a project budget; establish a project quality
control program; identify project risks; and define communication protocols. Eliminating all
project risk is not one of the six objectives.
TOPIC 4: PROJECT SCHEDULING TOOLS AND CONCEPTS
A Gantt chart is a bar chart with the horizontal axis representing a timescale and the vertical
axis listing work tasks and milestones. Horizontal bars represent task durations. It is the most
commonly used scheduling tool in design practice.
A Milestone List Schedule is a chronological list of due dates for critical or contract-defined
activities. It identifies key dates all parties must meet and is often included in Requests for
Proposals and project contracts.
The Critical Path Method (CPM) graphically shows dependent, interconnected, and related
activities. It identifies the critical path — the sequence of tasks that must stay on schedule for
the overall project to be completed on time. Tasks on the critical path have zero float.
PERT — Program Evaluation and Review Technique — is a work diagramming system that
uses statistics to determine the probabilities of task durations. It looks like circles or boxes
with connecting arrows and is used when task durations are uncertain.
Float, also called slack, is the amount of time a task can be delayed without affecting the
overall project completion date. A task on the critical path has zero float — any delay to it
delays the entire project.
Pass-the-Baton scheduling is a structure where one task must end before the next can begin
— also called a finish-to-start relationship. It creates sequential dependencies.
A Finish-to-Finish task relationship describes two or more tasks scheduled to finish at the
same time regardless of when they started. Used when parallel workstreams must converge
at the same milestone.
Two techniques for shortening project schedules are: running tasks in parallel rather than
sequentially, and using more resources by assigning additional staff to labor-intensive tasks.
The Percentage Complete method estimates drawing completion status. A drawing described
as fifty percent complete has been drawn but is not yet fully dimensioned and notes or
callouts are incomplete.
TOPIC 5: BUDGET MANAGEMENT AND EARNED VALUE
Actual Cost of Work Performed (ACWP) is the actual amount of money or hours spent
performing a task — what was actually spent.
Budgeted Cost of Work Performed (BCWP) is the amount of estimated budget earned based
on the work actually completed compared to the budget for that task. Also called earned
value. It answers: given how much work is complete, what should it have cost?
Budgeted Cost of Work Scheduled (BCWS) is the estimated or planned cost to complete a
task at a given point in time — what the work was supposed to cost according to the original
plan.
If ACWP is greater than BCWP, the project is over budget. If BCWP is greater than BCWS,
the project is ahead of schedule. If a team has billed 45 percent of the fee but produced only
30 percent of the work, this is an Earned Value red flag indicating budget is being consumed
faster than work is being produced.
A design contingency is a portion of the overall project budget — typically five to twenty
percent — reserved for genuinely unpredictable problems. It cannot be used to cover tasks
taking longer than expected due to inefficiency or poor estimating.
Budget recovery strategies include: writing a formal amendment if out-of-scope work is being
performed or stopping it immediately; reminding team members of their available budget; and
modifying the work plan to find more efficient methods.
Reimbursable expenses are amounts expended for or on account of the project and typically
include travel and subsistence expenses, communication expenses, reproduction costs such
as blueprinting and photocopying, subconsultant expenses for special services, and
identifiable production supply costs.
Materials and labor cost estimates should include labor and subcontract costs, material and
material waste, shipping, storage and staging, equipment and tools required for installation,
general conditions affecting cost such as insurance and bonding, design and project
management costs, and market or regional conditions that affect actual construction costs.
TOPIC 6: RISK MANAGEMENT
The five strategies for controlling project risk are: avoidance — eliminating the risk; prevention
— reducing the likelihood or severity; transfer — shifting the risk to another party through
contracts or insurance; mitigation — reducing the impact if the risk occurs; and contingency
planning — preparing a response in advance.
Contract-related risk management strategies include: using standardized contract forms
whenever possible; identifying excluded as well as included services to eliminate scope
ambiguity; and specifying dispute resolution methods in the contract — agreeing on mediation
or arbitration in advance is far less costly than litigation.
Non-contract risk management strategies include: informing clients of potential risks early;
choosing consultants carefully; not starting construction document production until the design
is well-defined and client-approved; and avoiding running projects without budget and
schedule contingency plans.
Standards of Practice are the guiding principles by which licensees conduct their day-to-day
responsibilities within their scope of practice. Reasonable care and skill means any work done
must be at least as good as the work of a competent person with average skills and
experience in the type of work required.
Professional Liability Insurance (Errors and Omissions insurance) protects from claims if a
client holds the professional responsible for errors or the failure of their work to perform as
promised. Coverage includes legal defense costs, judgments, and court costs. It is
claims-made, meaning coverage must be maintained continuously even after a project is
complete.
Courts apply the term design professional primarily to architects, engineers, landscape
architects, and surveyors. Design professionals are held to a standard of care consistent with
the minimum standard of care in their profession. Expert testimony is usually needed to
establish this standard.
TOPIC 7: COMMUNICATION AND PROFESSIONAL PRACTICE
Active listening is focusing on what is being said and how it is being said, asking follow-up
questions to fully understand the message, and formulating a response only after the
message has been completely understood.
Communication methods used to share knowledge and evaluate outcomes include:
pre-construction meetings, paper trail diary, proposals or contracts, material specifications,
schedules, financial information and vendor contracts, project status reports, signoff forms,
letters, memos and meeting agendas, conversations, interviews, computer software
programs, SMEE-mail archives, checklist and punch lists, legal documents such as deeds
and permits, telephone hotlines, key people contacts, and soils tests.
Best practices for professional meetings include: every meeting should have a clearly defined
purpose and desired outcome; an agenda should be distributed prior to the meeting and
followed during it; meetings should start and end on time; meetings should not exceed one
hour unless necessary; and someone should take minutes and distribute them after.
Techniques for facilitating meetings include: start and finish on time; set ground rules; include
only individuals who can contribute; make the meeting formal; move quickly through topics;
schedule breaks; and lead the meeting. Techniques for group discussion include the
discursive approach where discussion flows freely, the round robin approach where each
member speaks in turn, and the small group approach where the group breaks into smaller
focused groups.
The two most commonly used approaches to reach a group decision are the consensus
approach and the majority vote approach.
Meeting minutes are the official record of what discussions occurred and what decisions were
made. They record the action plan including tasks assigned, timelines for completion, and the
person responsible for each task.
Best practices for email include: use email to confirm discussions or decisions that have
already taken place; use email to transmit lengthy documents as attachments; avoid email for
sensitive conversations, complex negotiations, or delivering bad news — those require
face-to-face or phone communication.
The landscape architect's code of ethics addresses: responsibility to the public to protect
safety, health, property, and welfare; public statements and testimony based on knowledge
and competence; competency for assignments undertaken; conflicts of interest to be avoided;
improper solicitation of employment prohibited; knowledge of improper conduct by others
must not be concealed; and seals and rubber stamps affixed only to documents prepared by
or under the supervision of the licensee.
TOPIC 8: PROJECT PHASES AND TEAM MEMBER ROLES
Design phase components include: Planning phase — higher-level analysis, feasibility
studies, program, and budgeting; Schematic Design (SD) — a conceptual design based on
program and budget prepared for the owner including sketches, graphics, general design
criteria, and preliminary cost estimates; Design Development (DD) — follows owner approval
of SD with outline specifications, drawings, and estimates; Construction Documents (CDs) —
follows DD approval, the design team prepares documents for obtaining pricing; and Bidding
— the design team prepares clarifications to bid documents in the form of addenda.
Design and construction team member roles include: Owner — defines requirements and
provides approval at each phase; Architect — serves as lead design professional and
organizes design team activities and schedules; Engineer — prepares drawings,
specifications, and estimates; Consultant — prepares drawings, specifications, and estimates
as required by timeline and scope; Construction Manager (CM) — typically hired near end of
design phase, participates in final estimates, bid processes, and contractor selection; General
Contractor — not involved during design phase; Contractors and Subcontractors — not
involved during design phase.
Tasks during the construction phase include: periodic site observations to assure compliance
with contract documents; review of the complete document set; review of shop drawings;
processing submittals; monitoring the construction schedule; sampling materials; verification
of work completed; review and recommendations on pay applications; issuance of certificate
of substantial completion; and documents and modifications to construction administration
procedures as necessary.
Post-construction activities include: cutover and first-use activities; training of owner's
personnel on equipment use and adjustments; operation and maintenance of various
systems; and final payments and release of retainage, which are often made well after
occupancy once the owner is satisfied the project is fully completed.
The landscape architect's typical responsibilities include: defining scope and mutual
responsibilities; preparing work and fee schedules; surveys for future and existing facilities;
establishing priorities of development; preparing design and presentation drawings; making
preliminary cost estimates; developing the approved concept plan; making preliminary
material selections; drawing up preliminary construction details; updating cost estimates;
submitting detailed drawings and specifications for client approval; supplying bid documents;
reviewing bid quotations; assisting in award of contract; representing client to contractor in all
matters under contract; providing on-site supervision per contract; administering revisions and
shop drawings; and making final inspection at project conclusion.
Owner duties during construction include: disclose all property information needed to evaluate
or enforce lien rights; furnish reasonable evidence of sufficient financial arrangements; furnish
surveys showing physical characteristics, legal limitations, and utility locations; pay fees for all
permits, easements, and approvals; provide all information or services required by contract
documents; avoid any construction delay through reasonable promptness; pay for all copies
of drawings and specifications reasonably needed by the contractor; pay all sums when due;
and carry property insurance to protect the contractor's and owner's interests during
construction.
TOPIC 9: CONTRACT DOCUMENTS AND SPECIFICATIONS
A specification is an explicit set of requirements to be satisfied by a material, product, or
service. Specifications in North America form part of the contract documents that accompany
and govern the construction of a building or site.
The four main types of specifications are: Performance — focus on results, contractors
choose materials and methods to achieve desired results; Proprietary — call out brand names
and models; Descriptive — focus on exact properties and installation methods; and Reference
— requirements based on an established standard.
Three documents standardize construction format in North America, produced jointly by CSI
and Construction Specifications Canada (CSC): the Page Format standardizes how text on
pages in a specification is presented; the Section Format organizes information in each
section into Part 1 General, Part 2 Product, and Part 3 Execution; and the MasterFormat is a
master list of numbers and titles to organize activities and requirements of a construction
project including 16 divisions plus a summary of front-end requirements.
Drawings are the first of three elements of a design. They show the location of the work
required in relation to other required elements, as well as quantity and size. Drawings are
generally grouped by discipline: Structural (S), Architectural (A), Mechanical (M), Electrical
(E), and Plumbing (P). Types of drawings include Plan — horizontal views such as floor plans;
Elevation — vertical views such as wall or rack; Section — sectional view such as a sleeve
through a wall; Detail — details such as faceplate labeling; Schedule Spreadsheets — tables
of information; and General — symbols, legend, and notes.
The daily construction report is a written record of the day's activities including weather
conditions, work accomplished, number of personnel for each trade involved, equipment
involved, materials received, visitors or inspectors present, and problems encountered.
TOPIC 10: BIDDING AND BID DOCUMENTS
The owner typically supplies the bidding and contracting requirements to be included in the
project manual and often coordinates advertising or invitations to prospective bidders. The
architect, engineer, or consultant produces any addenda required to clarify contract
documents and may participate in evaluation or negotiation of bids. The Construction
Manager may lead or participate during bidding. The General Contractor, Contractor, and
Subcontractor prepare and submit bids to the owner.
Bid document components include: Bid solicitation — brief overview of scope, where
documents can be reviewed, bid security requirements, bid due date, time, and location;
Instructions to bidders — type of bid, how to prepare the bid, bid security, bonds and
certificates, permits, subcontractor listings, pre-bid meeting requirements, and information
related to bid opening; Information available to bidders — references to existing conditions,
hazardous materials, description of site and buildings; Bid response forms — project
identification, to whom bid is directed, who submitted it, acknowledgments, length of time bid
is valid, acknowledgment of addenda, pricing, start and completion dates, signature and date;
Contract forms; General conditions and supplementary conditions; Specifications; and
Drawings.
An addendum is a document that addresses changes or modifications made to the contract
and issued prior to the bidding process. It addresses questions, answers, errors, and changes
that evolve during bidding and is usually sent at least five days in advance of receipt of bids so
adjustments can be made.
A substitution is a process in which bidders request to use components other than those
specified in the contract. This usually occurs when there are proprietary specifications or few
manufacturers of the specified items. Approval results in an addendum sent to all bidders
informing them of acceptable substitution.
An alternate is a request included in the bidding documents that asks the contractor to provide
a price for some type of variation from the base bid, allowing flexibility in project cost.
Three types of construction contracts are: Stipulated Sum — a contractor submits a fixed
price for the scope of work required, most low-bid contracts are stipulated sum; Cost Plus Fee
— the contractor is reimbursed for actual costs plus a fee, a guaranteed maximum price
(GMP) may be required, and there can be a shared savings clause or incentives for early
completion; and Unit Price — used when actual scope cannot be determined at time of bid,
requires a realistic estimate of quantities for each activity type.
TOPIC 11: CONSTRUCTION CONTRACT ADMINISTRATION
Contract time refers to the period from the agreed upon starting date to the agreed upon
substantial completion date. It includes agreed upon adjustments and may be extended by
change order or circumstances beyond the contractor's control. Commencement is the
calendar specific time that states when the project will begin.
Substantial completion is the point at which the completion of the project is sufficient enough
but not completed so that the owner can occupy or use the site. It can be stated as a specific
calendar date or as a number of calendar days from commencement.
A change order form is completed when problems arise during construction, the client
changes their mind about what they want, or unforeseen additional work is discovered. The
change order details the new plan including services, materials, and designs that the client
and contractor have mutually agreed upon. The change order form then becomes the valid
contract.
Liquidated damages are specified daily charges deducted from moneys payable to the
contractor for each day the contractor fails to meet a milestone or contract completion date.
They are a contract-based remedy for late completion and must be agreed to by the parties in
the construction contract. Liquidated damages is defined as a sum which a party agrees to
pay or a deposit they agree to forfeit if they break a promise, having been arrived at by a good
faith effort to estimate in advance the actual damage that would probably ensue from the
breach.
Retainage is a percentage of the total bid which a contractor or subcontractor pays upon
submitting a bid for work. It is considered insurance and a good faith tool to assure the project
owner they are entering an agreement with a solvent, insured party who will be around for the
duration of the project. The money is held in escrow and paid back with interest when the
contractor has fulfilled obligations under the agreement.
Payment applications must have certification — a written statement of the correctness and
reliability of something and written permission to do something. They must be notarized,
submitted usually ten days before the date of each payment, include value of work done to the
date of application, include the value of any unused purchased materials in acceptable
storage, and must be approved, certified, and signed by the owner or designer before
payment.
Construction observation techniques include: observing installation to assure compliance with
contract documents; preparing change orders; resolving site and design issues; reviewing
submittals and making recommendations; preparing project documentation including daily
reports, photography, field measurements, construction notification memos, bi-monthly
summary reports, and meeting memos; monitoring all independent testing for material,
compaction, and utility compliance; preparing the punch list from outstanding work items;
informing the client if construction practices are contrary to the norm; notifying the client of
defects in the work of the contractor; and performing final review of the project.
Quality control procedures include: inspection by direct inspection of work products or
processes or by monitoring the activities creating the products or processes; tests to
determine if an item or service is acceptable or to acquire additional information; procedures
for handling, storage, and shipping of equipment or supplies critical to quality; and corrective
actions taken to ensure any conditions leading to failures, malfunctions, or deficiencies are
identified and corrected.
TOPIC 12: BONDS, INSURANCE, AND LEGAL CONCEPTS
A bid bond is submitted with a contractor's bid guaranteeing that if the bidder is awarded the
contract they will execute it and provide all required performance and payment bonds. It
protects the owner if the low bidder refuses to sign the contract after being selected.
A performance bond guarantees the faithful completion of the contractor's obligations under
the construction contract up to the face value of the bond. If the contractor defaults, the surety
completes the work or pays the cost of completion.
A payment bond guarantees that the contractor will pay all subcontractors, suppliers, and
laborers. It protects the owner from mechanics liens — unpaid parties claim against the bond
rather than against the owner's property.
Liquidated damages are a pre-agreed daily dollar amount charged to the contractor for each
day of delay past the contract completion date. They must be a genuine pre-estimate of the
owner's anticipated costs of delay, not an unreasonably high penalty. Courts will not enforce
liquidated damage clauses that function as punitive penalties.
Every surety bond involves three parties: the principal — the contractor who must perform; the
obligee — the owner to whom the obligation is owed; and the surety — the bonding company
that guarantees performance if the principal defaults.
General liability insurance protects against claims for bodily injury and property damage
arising from the contractor's operations. Workers compensation insurance provides medical
benefits and wage replacement to workers injured on the job. Builder's risk insurance, also
called course of construction insurance, covers the project itself against physical loss during
construction from events like fire, theft, and vandalism. Professional liability insurance, also
called errors and omissions insurance, covers claims arising from professional negligence in
the design work and is claims-made.
In arbitration, parties agree to give up their right to go to court and instead have disputes
resolved by an arbitrator or arbitration panel. In binding arbitration, parties agree to accept the
arbitrator's decision as final. In nonbinding arbitration, parties are free not to accept the
decision and may seek satisfaction through other means including a lawsuit. In mediation,
parties agree to enter into good faith negotiations through a neutral mediator in order to
attempt to resolve their differences.
A contract clause outlines how any disputes will be resolved. Most include an arbitration
agreement as a contractual provision and a mediation provision for good faith negotiations
through a neutral mediator.
In written warranty, there are two general types of warranties in construction: manufacturer
warranty of a product and labor warranty. A roofing contract may include a 30-year material
warranty and a 5-year labor warranty as an example.
TOPIC 13: PROJECT CLOSEOUT
Substantial completion is the status of a project when it is sufficiently complete to be occupied
and used by the owner for its intended purpose, even if minor work remains. It starts the
guarantee period, stops liquidated damages, and triggers partial retainage release.
A punch list is a written list of all remaining work items requiring completion or correction
before final completion. It is prepared during the substantial completion inspection. Each item
must be specific and unambiguous, identifying exactly where the problem is and exactly what
must be done.
Final completion is the status when all work including all punch list items has been completed
in full conformance with the contract documents. It triggers release of remaining retainage,
final payment, and submission of all closeout documents.
Closeout documents the contractor must submit include: as-built drawings showing the project
as actually constructed, operation and maintenance manuals for installed systems, warranties
and guarantees transferred to the owner, and a final lien waiver confirming all subcontractors
and suppliers have been paid in full.
The guarantee period, also called the warranty period, typically lasts one year from substantial
completion. During this period the contractor must return to the site and correct any defects or
failures in the completed work at no additional cost to the owner. It runs concurrently with but
is separate from manufacturer warranties on installed products.
Commissioning is the formal process of testing and verifying all installed systems — irrigation,
lighting, specialty equipment — to confirm they operate as designed. It is a quality verification
step performed at or near project closeout.
TOPIC 14: CONSTRUCTION PLANS AND DETAILS — 50% OF EXAM
A complete landscape architecture construction document set includes: cover sheet, existing
conditions plan, demolition plan, layout plan, materials plan, grading and drainage plan,
planting plan, irrigation plan, construction details, and supplemental plans as needed.
The cover sheet includes project name and address, client and consultant contact
information, property legal description, vicinity map, complete drawing list, permit information,
and applicable code references. The legal description is critical because permitting agencies
use it to locate the property in their records.
The existing conditions plan documents everything currently on the site including buildings,
trees, utilities, grades, pavement, property lines, and easements. It is prepared from a
topographic survey by a licensed surveyor.
The demolition plan shows what gets removed, what gets protected, and what remains. A
sawcut is a clean straight cut in existing pavement at the limit of demolition that maintains
structural integrity of the remaining pavement. The demolition plan must coordinate precisely
with the protection and mitigation plan.
The most important tree protection measure is construction barrier fencing at the dripline —
the outer edge of the canopy, which corresponds to the outer extent of the root zone. Fencing
must be installed before any site work begins. Inside the fenced zone no equipment may
operate, no materials may be stored, no soil may be compacted, and no grade changes may
occur.
DBH — diameter breast height — is measured at 4.5 feet above grade and is the standard
measurement for specifying tree size. Trees failing to meet specified DBH should be rejected
from the project.
The layout plan shows horizontal location of all new elements dimensioned from known
reference points. Two locating methods are used: dimensioning from fixed reference points
(works for rectilinear designs) and the coordinate method using northings and eastings
(preferred for large, complex, or curved projects).
The materials plan designates surface material for every area using hatching, keynotes, and a
legend. It must coordinate with construction details — the materials plan shows what material
goes where, the details show how it is built.
A retaining wall up to three feet can be designed by a landscape architect without a structural
engineer. Above three feet an engineer is required. Height is measured from bottom of footing
to top of wall. All retaining walls regardless of type must have drainage to prevent hydrostatic
pressure failure.
OSHA requires deck and platform railings to be at least 42 inches high. Maximum spacing
between railing members is 4 inches, based on the size of a baby's head. Plantings in site
triangles at 35 mph road entrances must be kept at or below 30 inches.
The three goals of landscape lighting are: facilitating safety, improving legibility of outdoor
spaces at night, and encouraging nighttime use. The most critical factor in recreational area
lighting is uniformity. The light source should never be directly visible to users. Low-voltage
lighting can be installed by a licensed landscape contractor. High-voltage lighting requires an
electrical permit and a licensed electrical contractor.
TOPIC 15: CONSTRUCTION SPECIFICATIONS AND BIDDING — 20% OF EXAM
The project manual contains four sections: bidding requirements, contract forms, conditions of
the contract (general and supplementary), and technical specifications. Together with
drawings these form the complete contract documents. When drawings and specifications
conflict, specifications typically govern.
General conditions are standardized documents outlining roles of all parties and procedures
for varying circumstances. Supplementary conditions are project-specific additions or
modifications. Supplementary conditions do not replace general conditions — they
supplement them.
CSI MasterFormat 2004 key divisions: Division 1 — General Requirements (administrative
and procedural requirements for the whole project); Division 31 — Earthwork (excavation,
grading, topsoil stripping and stockpiling); Division 32 — Exterior Improvements (paving, site
improvements, planting, and irrigation — irrigation moved here from Division 2 in the 1995
edition); Division 33 — Utilities (water, sewer, storm drainage outside buildings).
Technical specification sections follow three-part CSI format: Part 1 General — scope,
references to standards, submittals, quality assurance; Part 2 Products — materials,
products, and equipment to be used; Part 3 Execution — installation procedures, preparation,
tolerances, and field quality control.
Bid bonds are submitted with bids guaranteeing the bidder will execute the contract if
awarded. Addenda are formal written documents issued during bidding to clarify, correct, or
modify bidding documents. They must be issued to all bidders simultaneously and in writing
— verbal clarifications have no legal effect. Addenda become part of the contract documents.
Project delivery methods include: General Contract (design-bid-build) — designer and
contractor have separate contracts with owner; Design-Build — one entity contracts for both
design and construction; Professional Construction Management — CM firm represents
owner throughout design and construction; Multiple Prime Contracts — owner holds separate
contracts with multiple prime contractors.
TOPIC 16: CONSTRUCTION ADMINISTRATION — 30% OF EXAM
Pre-construction activities include: the pre-construction meeting covering scope, submittal
schedule, construction schedule, site logistics, safety requirements, and administrative
procedures; the pre-construction site walk to document existing conditions; installation of
survey control points before any earth is moved; and the Notice to Proceed (NTP) which starts
the contract duration clock and can be issued before the contract is formally signed.
An RFI — Request for Information — is a formal written inquiry from the contractor asking for
clarification about the construction documents. The landscape architect must respond
promptly, clearly, and definitively. Slow responses can delay construction and generate time
extension and delay cost claims. Vague responses that defer design decisions to the
contractor are inappropriate. Every RFI must be logged.
The landscape architect observes — not inspects or supervises — the work. Observation
means assessing whether work appears to be proceeding in general conformance with the
contract documents. Inspection implies control over means and methods and creates legal
liability. Contracts should use the word observation, never inspection or supervision.
A field report is the written record of each site observation. It must include: date, weather
conditions, persons present, description of work observed, deficiencies noted, verbal
instructions given to the contractor, open items requiring follow-up, and references to
photographs taken.
Submittals are documents provided by the contractor for review before materials are
incorporated into the project. Types include shop drawings, product data submittals, samples,
material certifications, manufacturer's literature, and mock-ups. The four response
designations are: Approved, Approved as Noted, Revise and Resubmit, and Rejected. The
landscape architect's approval does not relieve the contractor of responsibility for conforming
to the contract documents.
Shop drawings are prepared by the contractor, subcontractor, or fabricator — not the
landscape architect — showing how a specific item will be manufactured and installed. They
are submitted before fabrication. As-built drawings are produced after construction showing
the project as actually built.
A substitution request is a formal request to use a different product from what was specified.
Cost alone does not justify approval — the substitute must meet technical and aesthetic
requirements. Or-equal clauses in specifications allow substitutions that are genuinely equal
in quality and performance.
A mock-up is a full or partial scale physical sample of a work element built on site for review
before that work proceeds. Once approved it becomes the quality standard for all subsequent
work of that type. Mock-ups reveal construction challenges early, establish clear visual
standards, and create contractor accountability.
A change order is a formal written amendment to the construction contract signed by both
parties before changed work proceeds. A field change (field order) is a minor modification not
affecting contract amount or duration. A change directive authorizes the contractor to proceed
with changed work when time is critical, with price to be determined afterward. A bulletin is a
written document issued during construction to clarify or modify the contract documents — the
construction-phase equivalent of an addendum.
The three sources of change orders are: changes in owner scope or program, design errors or
omissions, and differing site conditions. Differing site conditions are physical conditions that
differ from what was shown in the bidding documents or from what would reasonably be
expected. They do not terminate the contract — they are resolved through the change order
process.
Owner-caused delays entitle the contractor to both a time extension and additional
compensation for delay costs. Excusable delays (force majeure) entitle the contractor to a
time extension only with no additional compensation. Contractor-caused delays entitle the
contractor to neither — the owner may assess liquidated damages.
Construction acceleration types: Actual acceleration is an explicit written directive from the
owner to speed up. Constructive acceleration occurs when the owner denies a legitimate time
extension request, forcing the contractor to speed up without explicit direction — still entitles
the contractor to compensation. Fast-tracking overlaps design and construction phases to
compress overall schedule — not the same as acceleration.
Retainage is a percentage — typically five to ten percent — of each progress payment
withheld by the owner throughout construction. Released at or after final completion. Its
purpose is to give the contractor financial incentive to complete all work and punch list items.
Project management roles: the Project Manager leads the team and is responsible for scope,
schedule, and budget; the Project Controller manages project finances and works with the PM
to keep the project financially on track; the Construction Administrator manages the
construction phase including site observations, RFIs, submittals, change orders, and closeout.
OAC meetings — owner-architect-contractor — are regular coordination meetings held during
construction, typically monthly or biweekly, with the landscape architect preparing the agenda
and minutes.
TOPIC 17: PROJECT ACCOUNTING AND COST ESTIMATING
Salary-related expenses, also called burden costs, are additional labor costs beyond base
salary including employer contributions to Social Security, Medicare, unemployment
insurance, workers compensation, health insurance, vacation pay, and sick leave. They are a
direct cost of employment and must be included when calculating the true cost of labor.
Other Direct Costs (ODCs) are miscellaneous project-related costs other than direct labor,
typically billed as reimbursable expenses. Examples include postage, printing and
reproduction, messenger services, travel expenses, and photo development.
RS Means and Saylor publish cost estimating reference manuals for the United States. CSI
publishes specification standards. ASTM publishes material testing standards. Neither CSI
nor ASTM publishes cost estimating data.
A budgetary cost model breaks down estimated construction costs into major project
components organized by CSI MasterFormat number. It establishes cost targets for each
component and provides a baseline for evaluating different materials and construction
approaches.
Program validation is the design team's independent review of the client's stated program and
budget before design begins, identifying cost increases or unanticipated requirements. It
prevents investing design effort in a program that cannot be built within budget.
Design to Cost is a method in which a cost target is set at the outset and all subsequent
design decisions are monitored for conformance with that target. Cost management is
integrated into the design process from the beginning.
Value Engineering is a review of a project by a third-party team of experts aimed at improving
performance, reliability, quality, safety, and life-cycle costs. It is an independent third-party
process distinct from internal quality control.
Shell package is designed space delivered in a basic unfinished condition with the developer
providing structure and core systems but not interior build-out. Tenant improvement package
is design and construction work paid for by tenants who wish to customize their space.
