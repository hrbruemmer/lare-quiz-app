TOPIC: PRE-CONSTRUCTION ACTIVITIES

Pre-construction activities are the steps that take place between contract execution and the start of physical construction work.

The pre-construction meeting:
- First formal gathering of all parties after the contract is signed and before any site work begins
- Landscape architect typically facilitates this meeting
- Landscape architect prepares and distributes meeting minutes promptly after

Attendees at the pre-construction meeting typically include:
- Owner or owner's representative
- Landscape architect or project architect of record
- General contractor
- Key subcontractors
- Local building or permitting authority (sometimes)

Items covered at the pre-construction meeting:
- Project scope review — everyone starts with the same understanding of what is being built
- Submittal schedule — detailed list of all required submittals, who is responsible, when each must be submitted and returned
- Construction schedule including key milestones and contract completion date
- Site logistics — where materials will be staged, where the site office will be located, how access will be managed, what security measures are required
- Safety requirements and applicable OSHA standards
- Administrative procedures for RFIs, change orders, pay applications, and substitution requests
- Communication chain and chain of command
- Survey control point locations and protection requirements

Pre-construction site walk:
- Physical walk of the site with the owner and contractor before any work begins
- Primary purpose: document existing conditions of the site and all adjacent areas
- Documents through notes and photographs
- Creates a baseline record before any work starts
- Protects all parties if damage claims arise during or after construction
- If a neighbor claims damage was caused by construction, the documentation establishes what conditions existed before work began
- Without this documentation there is no baseline to compare against

Survey control points:
- Fixed reference markers from which all construction staking is referenced
- Establish the coordinate system for the project
- MUST be installed before any clearing, demolition, or grading begins — before any earth is moved
- If disturbed by early construction activity before use, the entire project positioning can be compromised
- Critical for: locating walls, paths, structures, utilities, and all constructed elements
- Used throughout construction from clearing to grading to building locations to irrigation connections

Notice to Proceed (NTP):
- The written document by which the owner formally authorizes the contractor to begin work
- The NTP date is the reference point from which contract duration is measured
- Can be issued BEFORE the contract is formally signed if the owner needs work to begin immediately
- Once issued, the contractor can mobilize and begin work

Commonly required forms during construction administration:
- Notice to Proceed: tells the contractor to begin work as of a particular date
- Meeting minutes: keeps track of issues raised in meetings and forms the agenda for subsequent meetings
- Request for Information (RFI): used to obtain a formal response to the contract documents
- Field orders: used to facilitate minor changes to the requirements that do not require a change order
- Change order requests: can be submitted by the contractor in response to a field order or as an uninitiated request for a change in scope
- Change order: the formal document that defines what changes in project scope are required and identifies the associated changes to time frame, dollar amounts, or both
- Punch list: a document that summarizes corrections that a contractor must make prior to the work being accepted as complete

TOPIC: REQUESTS FOR INFORMATION (RFIs)

An RFI — Request for Information — is a formal written inquiry submitted by the contractor to the landscape architect asking for clarification or additional information about the construction documents.

RFIs arise when the contractor encounters:
- Something unclear, ambiguous, or missing from the documents
- An apparent error or inconsistency (dimension on one sheet does not match another)
- A conflict between the specifications and the detail drawings
- A field condition not reflected in the documents (existing utility not shown on drawings)
- A question about the designer's intent for a specific detail
- A condition simply not anticipated in the documents

The landscape architect's three required response qualities:
1. PROMPT — respond within the timeframe established in the contract (typically a defined number of business days from receipt)
2. CLEAR — leave no room for misinterpretation; the landscape architect must make the design decision, not defer it to the contractor
3. DEFINITIVE — final and actionable so the contractor knows exactly what to do next

Legal consequences of slow RFI responses:
- May delay the contractor's work
- Contractor may be entitled to a time extension
- Contractor may be entitled to additional compensation for delay costs
- The designer has an implied professional duty to respond promptly

Legal consequences of vague RFI responses:
- Transfers design decision-making authority to the contractor
- The contractor is not a licensed design professional
- Design decisions are the landscape architect's responsibility
- Can create professional liability if the contractor's decision leads to a problem

RFI log requirements:
- Must be maintained for every RFI
- Records: RFI number, date received, submitter, question description, response date, response summary
- Serves as the project's documented record of all questions and answers during construction
- Primary reference document if disputes arise about when issues were identified or how they were resolved

When an RFI response involves a design change:
- May need to be followed up with a formal drawing revision or bulletin
- Must be incorporated into the project record
- All revised documents issued during construction must be logged

High volume of RFIs signals:
- Quality issues in the construction documents (ambiguities, inconsistencies, or missing information)
- Analyzing RFI patterns after a project helps improve future document quality

TOPIC: MANAGING THE CONSTRUCTION CONTRACT

Budget management during construction:

Retainage:
- A percentage of each progress payment withheld by the owner throughout construction
- Typically 5 to 10 percent of each progress payment
- Held throughout the construction period
- Generally returned after final completion
- Purpose: gives the contractor financial incentive to complete all work and address all punch list items
- From the contractor's perspective: cash earned but not collectible until the job is finished
- At substantial completion: a portion (often half) may be released
- At final completion: remaining retainage is released

Pay application review:
- Landscape architect reviews the contractor's monthly pay application
- Compares claimed completion percentages against site observation records
- Must certify the amount that should be paid based on professional judgment
- Certifying payment for work not done exposes the landscape architect to professional liability
- Should cross-reference claimed work against field reports and site observation notes
- Should confirm all required submittals for that work have been received and approved before certifying payment

Stored materials in pay applications:
- Contractor may include materials purchased but not yet installed
- Materials must be properly stored, protected, and documented
- Landscape architect must verify documentation and protection before certifying payment

TOPIC: CHANGE ORDERS

A change order is:
- A formal written amendment to the construction contract
- Modifies scope of work, contract price, contract duration, or any combination
- MUST be agreed upon and signed by BOTH the owner and contractor BEFORE the changed work is performed
- The mutual agreement and prior written authorization distinguishes a change order from other modifications

The three primary sources of change orders:
1. Changes in the owner's program or desires (owner wants something added, removed, or changed)
2. Design errors or omissions (documents contained a mistake or left something out)
3. Differing site conditions (actual field conditions differ from what was shown in bidding documents)

Change order process sequence:
1. Contractor identifies a condition requiring a change
2. Contractor submits a change order request (change order proposal) with detailed cost and time breakdown
3. Landscape architect reviews the proposal for reasonableness
4. Landscape architect checks if the work is truly outside the original contract scope
5. Landscape architect makes a recommendation to the owner
6. Owner approves, denies, or negotiates
7. Both parties sign the change order
8. Changed work proceeds

CRITICAL PRINCIPLE: Changed work must NEVER proceed without written authorization.
- Verbal directions do not authorize changed work — even from the owner or landscape architect
- If contractor performs additional work based on a verbal direction without a signed change order, collecting payment is extremely difficult
- This is one of the most common sources of construction disputes
- Best practice: never allow changed work to proceed based solely on a verbal instruction

When evaluating change order cost proposals:
- Compare proposed pricing against original unit prices in the bid form
- Compare against published cost data from RS Means or similar references
- Use professional judgment about what the work should cost
- Contractors sometimes price change orders higher than their original bid because the owner is in a weaker negotiating position
- The landscape architect's role is to protect the owner's interests by ensuring fair pricing

Differing site conditions:
- Physical conditions encountered during construction that differ from what was shown in the bidding documents OR
- Conditions that differ from what would reasonably be expected at a site of that type
- Examples: unexpected underground utilities, buried structures, rock where soil was expected, groundwater at unexpected level
- Do NOT automatically terminate the contract
- Are addressed through the change order process
- Contractor documents the condition, submits a change order request for additional time and cost
- Parties negotiate a resolution

TOPIC: BULLETINS

A bulletin (also called a construction bulletin or clarification bulletin):
- A written document issued by the landscape architect during construction
- Purpose: clarify, supplement, or modify the construction documents
- The primary tool for formally communicating document changes to the contractor during construction
- The construction-phase equivalent of an addendum (which is issued during bidding)

Key difference between addenda and bulletins:
- Addenda: issued during the BIDDING period before the contract is signed
- Bulletins: issued during CONSTRUCTION after the contract is executed

Common reasons for issuing a bulletin:
- Clarifying a detail that was unclear in the original documents
- Providing additional information in response to an RFI that requires revised drawings
- Issuing revised drawings incorporating owner-directed changes
- Providing supplemental details for conditions not fully detailed in the original set

Bulletin rules:
- Numbered sequentially (Bulletin No. 1, No. 2, etc.)
- Logged as part of the project record
- Every bulletin becomes part of the project record
- In most cases bulletins become part of the contract documents
- Contractor is obligated to build in accordance with the most current bulletin for any given element

Critical distinction — when a bulletin requires a change order:
- A bulletin that CLARIFIES existing contract documents without changing scope: does NOT require a change order
- A bulletin that ADDS scope, changes materials to something more expensive, or modifies work in a way that affects the contractor's cost or schedule: MUST be accompanied by a corresponding change order
- Issuing a scope-adding bulletin without a change order asks the contractor to do more work without compensation — this is both unfair and legally untenable

TOPIC: PURCHASE REQUESTS

A purchase request (also called a purchase order):
- A formal document used to authorize the purchase of specific materials, products, or equipment
- Used in two primary contexts in construction administration

Context 1 — Owner-furnished, contractor-installed work:
- When the owner is supplying certain materials or equipment directly
- Landscape architect may be involved in preparing or reviewing purchase requests for owner-supplied items
- Documents exactly what is being ordered, from whom, for what price, and on what schedule

Context 2 — Long-lead items:
- Materials or equipment with extended manufacturing and delivery times
- Must be ordered early in construction to avoid project delays
- Contractor submits purchase requests before ordering long-lead items
- Landscape architect reviews to verify the correct product is being ordered per specifications
- Landscape architect verifies timing is consistent with the construction schedule
- Common long-lead landscape items: custom site furnishings, specialty lighting fixtures, large specimen trees, custom fabricated elements

Why purchase request review matters:
- Catching errors before ordering is far less costly than after delivery
- Gives owner visibility into what is being incorporated before money is spent
- Allows early identification of products that do not meet specifications

TOPIC: CHANGE DIRECTIVES

A change directive (also called a construction change directive or interim directive):
- A written instruction from the owner, issued through the landscape architect
- Directs the contractor to proceed with specific changed work
- Used when time is critical and waiting to negotiate a full change order would cause unacceptable delay
- Price is NOT agreed upon before work begins

THE KEY DIFFERENCE between a change order and a change directive:
- Change order: requires mutual agreement on price and time BEFORE work proceeds — both parties sign first
- Change directive: authorizes the contractor to proceed IMMEDIATELY, with price to be determined and formalized in a change order AFTER the work is complete

When change directives are used:
- An unforeseen condition requires an immediate response (unexpected underground rock must be removed to continue grading)
- The owner needs immediate action but does not have time to negotiate a full change order
- Time constraints would cause costly delays if the change order process was followed in full

Contractor's obligations under a change directive:
- Must meticulously document ALL costs associated with the directed work
- Every hour of labor, piece of equipment, and material must be tracked and recorded
- This documentation becomes the basis for the subsequent change order negotiation
- A contractor who performs change directive work without detailed cost records may be unable to recover full costs

Change directives must ALWAYS be in writing:
- Even in urgent field situations, a verbal direction must be followed immediately by a written change directive
- Never allow significant changed work to proceed based solely on verbal instruction, even in an emergency

TOPIC: SUBMITTALS — OVERVIEW AND PROCESS

Submittals are documents, samples, and physical items provided by the contractor to the landscape architect for review and approval before certain materials, products, or systems are incorporated into the project.

Purpose of submittal review:
- Verify before materials are installed that the contractor intends to use products and methods that comply with the contract documents
- One of the landscape architect's most important quality control responsibilities during construction
- Primary opportunity to prevent non-conforming materials from being incorporated into the project

Submittal schedule:
- Established at the pre-construction meeting
- Lists every required submittal, who is responsible, required submission date, and required return date
- Landscape architect must review submittals within the timeframe specified in the contract (typically 14 to 21 calendar days)
- Late returns delay the contractor's ability to order materials
- Late returns can cause construction delays for which the landscape architect may bear responsibility

Types of submittals:
- Shop drawings
- Product data submittals (materials submittals)
- Samples
- Material certifications
- Manufacturer's literature
- Mock-ups
- Test reports
- Manuals and user instructions

Items the contractor submits during the construction phase:
- Certificates of insurance
- Surety bonds
- List of subcontractors and product lists
- Schedules of activities
- Shop drawings
- Product data
- Samples
- Test reports
- Manuals and user manuals
- Project photographs

Submittal response designations — know all four:
1. APPROVED — conforms to the contract documents as submitted; contractor may proceed with fabrication or purchase as shown
2. APPROVED AS NOTED — acceptable with specific modifications noted on the returned document; contractor must incorporate modifications before proceeding; resubmission not required unless modifications are significant
3. REVISE AND RESUBMIT — does not conform to contract documents; must be substantially revised and resubmitted for another review round; contractor cannot proceed until approved
4. REJECTED — fundamentally unacceptable and incompatible with contract requirements

CRITICAL PRINCIPLE — the most important concept in submittal review:
- The landscape architect's approval of a submittal does NOT relieve the contractor of their responsibility to ensure the work conforms to the contract documents
- Approval confirms the proposed design and materials APPEAR to conform to the contract documents as the landscape architect understands them
- Approval does NOT certify dimensional accuracy
- Approval does NOT certify fabrication correctness
- Approval does NOT mean compliance with construction means and methods
- If fabricated work contains dimensional errors not caught in review, that is the CONTRACTOR'S responsibility
- This is one of the most heavily and directly tested concepts on the exam

TOPIC: SHOP DRAWINGS

Shop drawings definition:
- Drawings prepared by the contractor, subcontractor, manufacturer, or fabricator
- NOT prepared by the landscape architect
- Show exactly how a specific item will be manufactured, fabricated, and installed
- Translate the landscape architect's design intent into production-ready instructions for the fabricator

Example of a shop drawing:
- A steel fabricator preparing to build a custom gate submits shop drawings showing: exact dimensions of every member, connection details at every joint, weld sizes and types, surface treatment and finish, anchor bolt pattern, and sequence of assembly

When shop drawings are submitted:
- BEFORE fabrication — before the item is manufactured, cut, welded, or finished
- This is the entire point: catching errors on paper costs almost nothing
- Catching errors after fabrication — when the item arrives on site and does not fit — can cost tens of thousands of dollars

Shop drawings vs. as-built drawings:
- Shop drawings: submitted BEFORE fabrication, show how item will be made and installed
- As-built drawings: produced AFTER construction is complete, show the project as actually built incorporating all field changes
- As-built drawings are a contract CLOSEOUT document, not a pre-construction submittal

The landscape architect's role in reviewing shop drawings:
- Reviews for conformance with the design intent and contract documents
- Verifies that the fabricator's interpretation matches the design intent
- Does NOT check for dimensional accuracy — that is the contractor's responsibility
- Does NOT certify that fabrication is correct
- Does NOT take responsibility for means and methods of construction

TOPIC: MATERIALS SUBMITTALS AND SAMPLES

Materials submittals (product data submittals):
- Manufacturer-prepared technical data sheets, catalog cuts, installation instructions, and performance data
- For specific products the contractor proposes to use
- Landscape architect compares product data against specification requirements

What the landscape architect verifies in a materials submittal:
- That the proposed product's technical performance values meet specification requirements
- Examples of values checked: compressive strength, absorption rate, freeze-thaw resistance, slip resistance, color and finish consistency
- Creates a documented record of exactly what products were approved for incorporation

Samples:
- Physical examples of materials submitted for review and approval
- Examples: piece of paving stone, paint chip, fabric swatch, mortar sample, plant material sample
- Approved samples become the standard of comparison for materials delivered to the site
- If delivered materials do not match the approved sample, the landscape architect has grounds to reject them
- Samples are commonly required for: paving materials, stone, wood finishes, site furnishing finishes, plant materials

Material certifications:
- Documents from the manufacturer certifying that a product meets specified standards
- Example: a certification that concrete pipe meets ASTM standards

Test reports:
- Laboratory or field test results confirming product performance
- Example: compressive strength test results for concrete, soil compaction test results

TOPIC: SUBSTITUTION REQUESTS

A substitution request:
- A formal request from the contractor to use a product or material different from what was specified
- Must follow the process defined in Division 1 of the specifications
- Typically must be submitted within a defined period after contract execution
- Late substitution requests that could have been avoided by timely ordering are generally not acceptable

When substitution requests arise:
- The specified product is unavailable or discontinued
- The specified product has an unacceptable lead time
- The contractor believes an equivalent product is available at lower cost
- A specified product is no longer manufactured

Required contents of a substitution request:
- Complete technical information about the proposed substitute
- Side-by-side comparison showing it meets the specified product's performance requirements
- Information about any impact on other work in the project
- Pricing information
- Evidence that the substitute is actually equivalent

Approval criteria for substitution requests:
- The substitute must meet the TECHNICAL requirements of the specification
- The substitute must meet the AESTHETIC requirements of the specification
- Cost alone is NOT sufficient justification for approval
- If a substitute is equivalent in performance and aesthetics but costs less, those savings typically belong to the OWNER — not the contractor — unless the contract states otherwise
- The landscape architect should not approve a substitution simply because it is cheaper

Or-equal clauses vs. substitution requests:
- Or-equal clause: in the specification itself, lists a named product followed by "or equal" or "or approved equal" — contractor may use any product the landscape architect determines is genuinely equal
- Substitution request: a separate formal process required when a product is specified without an or-equal option
- Or-equal clause builds flexibility into the spec; substitution request is the process when that flexibility is not built in

TOPIC: MOCK-UPS

A mock-up:
- A full-scale or partial-scale physical sample of a work element
- Assembled in place on the PROJECT SITE (not in a factory or off-site)
- Built by the contractor for review and approval by the landscape architect and owner
- Must be reviewed BEFORE that work proceeds in full across the project

When mock-ups are required:
- Work elements where quality, appearance, and technique are critical
- When reviewing flat product data or small samples is not sufficient to evaluate the finished result

Common landscape architecture applications for mock-ups:
- Stone or masonry wall sections showing actual coursing pattern, joint width, mortar color, and surface finish
- Complex paving layouts showing pattern, joint alignment, and edge treatment
- Specialty concrete finishes such as exposed aggregate, broom finish, or stamped patterns
- Planting bed installations showing mulch depth, plant spacing, and edge definition
- Specialty site furnishing installations
- Any custom or complex work where appearance is critical to the design intent

Mock-up review process:
1. Contractor assembles the mock-up per specifications
2. Contractor submits the mock-up for review
3. Landscape architect and owner evaluate it IN PERSON — not from photographs
4. Evaluate for conformance with design intent
5. If approved: mock-up becomes the quality standard for all subsequent work of that type on the project
6. If not approved: contractor revises and resubmits

Once approved, the mock-up becomes:
- The quality standard against which all subsequent work of that type is evaluated
- Every subsequent installation of that work element must match the approved mock-up in quality, appearance, and technique
- The contractor cannot claim they did not know the expected standard

The three purposes of mock-ups:
1. Reveal practical construction challenges EARLY before they become widespread and expensive problems
2. Establish clear visual quality standards — reduce subjectivity in quality evaluation
3. Create contractor ACCOUNTABILITY — once approved, the expected standard is defined

What the contract documents should specify about mock-ups:
- Which elements require mock-ups
- Minimum size or extent of the mock-up
- The review procedure
- The disposition of the mock-up at project completion (incorporated into work, retained as reference, or removed)

TOPIC: CONSTRUCTION SEQUENCING

The proper sequencing of a landscape construction project:
1. Receive bids and award the project
2. Perform mass earthwork — rough grading, clearing, and demolition
3. Install minor site structures — walls, foundations, drainage structures, and underground utilities
4. Install irrigation and plants

Why this sequence must be followed:
- Cannot install irrigation before rough grading because grades determine where pipes run
- Cannot plant before irrigation is in because plants need water immediately after installation
- Cannot do mass earthwork while plants are in the ground
- Each step depends on completion of the one before it

Critical note on irrigation sleeves:
- Must be installed AFTER mass earthwork
- Must be installed BEFORE any permanent hardscape construction — before paving, concrete, or other site construction
- Allows irrigation pipes and control wires to be threaded under hardscape later without demolishing finished work
- Missing this timing window is a very costly mistake requiring demolition of finished hardscape

Construction surveying:
- Critical for locating building corners, grades, utility connections, and other site elements
- Coordinate type used to locate construction elements: NORTHINGS AND EASTINGS (a Cartesian coordinate system)

Plane surveying:
- Method typically used on small to medium landscape architecture projects
- Measures distances along the horizontal plane
- Disregards the curvature of the earth
- Modern plane surveying uses a laser to verify and measure distances and elevations
- The instrument stores information digitally, downloading it to a program that creates a topographic map
- Foresights and backsights are used to verify the surveyor's location

Geodetic surveying:
- Takes into account the curvature of the earth
- Used for large-scale projects or projects crossing vast areas of land
- Used for national mapping and large infrastructure projects
- Example: NOAA would use geodetic survey to map a section of the Bering Sea

GPS (Global Positioning System) for large-scale staking:
- Most economical method for large-scale construction staking such as state highway projects
- GPS units are portable, give accurate readings
- Do not require line-of-sight measurements
- Can be operated by a very small crew or even one person
- Can be done by a GPS unit, a laser transit, a survey crew, or links/Philadelphia rod depending on project scale

Bearing in surveying:
- Always taken with north or south as the starting direction depending on which hemisphere
- Most road and building projects use a northings and eastings coordinate system

ALTA survey:
- Used when a project consists of multiple irregular parcels the owner wants to consolidate
- Boundary line adjustment can also be used for consolidating parcels
- A topographic survey shows existing grades and features
- GPS mapping is used for large areas

Survey control points are typically installed:
- Prior to site clearing — before any disturbance
- These are critical reference markers for all construction staking throughout the project
- They are essential for liability, for potential change orders, and for verification of all project components from clearing to grading to irrigation system connections

Hub and whisker stakes:
- Used in surveying to set the base elevation on a site
- Hub is driven into the ground
- Whisker (small nail or stake) is set at the exact elevation
- Used to relate information to adjoining properties

Benchmark:
- Used to set elevations related to a landscape architecture project
- All topographic surveys indicate the datum and benchmark that the survey is tied to
- Used as the base reference point for designing project grades and elevations

Corner pin:
- A surveying monument marking a property corner
- Not the same as a benchmark

TOPIC: SCHEDULING TOOLS IN CONSTRUCTION ADMINISTRATION

Gantt chart: bar chart with horizontal axis as timescale and vertical axis listing tasks and milestones, bars represent task durations — most common scheduling tool

Critical Path Method (CPM): graphically shows dependent activities and identifies the critical path — the sequence that must stay on schedule for overall project to finish on time

PERT (Program Evaluation and Review Technique): uses statistics to determine probability of task durations — looks like circles or boxes with connecting arrows — used when durations are uncertain

Float (slack): amount of time a task can be delayed without affecting overall completion — zero float means the task is on the critical path

Milestone List Schedule: chronological list of critical due dates — often included in RFPs and contracts

Pass-the-Baton scheduling (finish-to-start): one task must end before the next begins

Finish-to-Finish relationship: two tasks scheduled to finish at the same time

Techniques for schedule compression:
- Run tasks in parallel rather than sequentially
- Add additional staff to labor-intensive tasks

Percentage Complete method: 50 percent complete means drawn but not yet fully dimensioned with notes and callouts incomplete

TOPIC: BUDGET MONITORING TOOLS IN CONSTRUCTION ADMINISTRATION

ACWP — Actual Cost of Work Performed: what was actually spent
BCWP — Budgeted Cost of Work Performed: earned value — what completed work should have cost
BCWS — Budgeted Cost of Work Scheduled: planned cost — what the work was supposed to cost at this point

Red flag: if team has billed 45 percent of fee but produced only 30 percent of work — budget is being consumed faster than work is produced

Design contingency:
- 5 to 20 percent of total budget
- Reserved for genuinely unpredictable events
- NOT for tasks taking longer than expected due to inefficiency

Budget recovery strategies:
- Write formal amendment if out-of-scope work is being performed, or stop it immediately
- Remind team members of their available budget
- Modify work plan to find more efficient methods

Percentage Complete method: 50 percent complete = plans drawn but not fully dimensioned, notes or callouts incomplete
